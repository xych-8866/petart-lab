📊 Fact: Normal body temperature for dogs and cats ranges from 100.5°F to 102.5°F (38.1°C to 39.2°C). A temperature above 103.5°F is considered a fever and warrants veterinary attention.

2. Signs of Fever

  • Warm ears and paws – often the first noticeable sign.
  • Lethargy – less active, sleeping more.
  • Loss of appetite – refusing food or water.
  • Shivering – despite being warm to the touch.
  • Panting or rapid breathing – trying to cool down.
  • Vomiting or diarrhea – may accompany fever.
⚠️ A temperature above 106°F is a life‑threatening emergency. Seek immediate veterinary care.

3. How to Take Your Pet's Temperature

Rectal (Gold Standard, Most Accurate)

  • Use a digital rectal thermometer specifically for pets (or a flexible human thermometer).
  • Lubricate the tip with petroleum jelly or K‑Y jelly.
  • Gently insert about 1 inch (for cats/small dogs) or 1‑2 inches (for large dogs).
  • Wait for the beep (about 30 seconds).
  • Clean the thermometer after use.

Ear (Infrared) – Less Accurate but Easier

  • Use an infrared ear thermometer designed for pets.
  • Point directly into the ear canal and read the temperature.
  • Less invasive, but may be less accurate.

Axillary (Under Armpit) – Least Accurate, but Gentle

  • Place the thermometer under the armpit, close the leg, and hold for 2‑3 minutes.
  • Add 0.5‑1°F to get an approximate core temperature (less reliable).

4. Common Causes of Fever

  • Infections – bacterial (urinary, skin, respiratory), viral, or fungal.
  • Inflammation – pancreatitis, arthritis, immune‑mediated diseases.
  • Heatstroke – elevated body temperature due to environmental heat.
  • Toxins – ingestion of toxic substances.
  • Vaccine reaction – mild fever is common after vaccination.

5. When to See the Vet

  • Temperature > 103.5°F lasting more than 24 hours.
  • Temperature > 105°F – immediate emergency.
  • Pet is lethargic, not eating, or showing signs of pain.
  • Pet is very young, very old, or has pre‑existing health conditions.

7. What NOT to Do

  • Do not give human fever reducers (ibuprofen, acetaminophen, aspirin) – they are toxic to pets.
  • Do not put your pet in cold water – can cause shock.
  • Do not wait too long – fever is a sign of illness; early treatment improves outcomes.

8. Your Action Plan

  1. Learn to take your pet's temperature (practice when they're healthy).
  2. If you notice signs of fever, take a temperature reading.
  3. If temperature is > 103.5°F, contact your vet.
  4. If temperature is > 105°F, go to an emergency vet immediately.
