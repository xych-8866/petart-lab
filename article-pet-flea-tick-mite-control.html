📊 Prevalence: Fleas are the most common external parasite in dogs and cats. A single flea can lay up to 50 eggs per day, leading to rapid infestations.

1. Types of External Parasites

  • Fleas – tiny, dark brown insects that jump. Cause itching, hair loss, and tapeworms. Visible as "flea dirt" (black specks that turn red on wet paper towel).
  • Ticks – larger, dark, eight‑legged arachnids that attach to the skin and feed on blood. Can transmit Lyme disease, ehrlichiosis, and anaplasmosis.
  • Mites – microscopic parasites (ear mites, sarcoptic mange, demodectic mange). Cause intense itching, ear debris, and skin scaling.

2. Signs of Infestation

  • Excessive scratching, licking, or biting – especially around the tail base, neck, and groin.
  • Hair loss or scabs – from allergic reactions to parasite saliva.
  • Black specks on skin or bedding – flea dirt (dried blood).
  • Visible ticks – often found around the head, ears, or between toes.
  • Head shaking or ear debris – signs of ear mites (especially in cats).
⚠️ If your pet is infested, treat both the pet and the environment. Most flea and mite infestations originate from the home environment (carpets, bedding, furniture).

3. Prevention is Better Than Treatment

Year‑round prevention is the most effective way to protect your pet. Common options include:

  • Topical treatments – applied to the skin between the shoulder blades (e.g., Revolution, Frontline Plus).
  • Oral medications – chewable tablets that kill fleas and ticks quickly (e.g., NexGard, Bravecto).
  • Collars – provide long‑acting protection (e.g., Seresto).

4. Treating an Existing Infestation

  • Step 1: Treat your pet – use a vet‑approved topical, oral, or collar product.
  • Step 2: Treat the environment – vacuum carpets, wash bedding in hot water, and use an environmental spray.
  • Step 3: Continue prevention – maintain year‑round protection to prevent re‑infestation.
