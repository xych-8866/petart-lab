1. Understanding Arthritis: More Than Just Pain

Osteoarthritis (OA) is a chronic, progressive disease affecting the joints. It involves cartilage degradation, inflammation, and changes in the joint structure. Over time, it causes pain, stiffness, and reduced mobility. Early intervention is key to slowing progression.

2. Physical Therapy and Rehabilitation

Range of Motion (ROM) Exercises

Gentle, passive exercises help maintain joint flexibility and prevent stiffness. Perform daily for 5‑10 minutes:

  • Flexion and extension: Gently flex and extend the knee, elbow, and hip joints in a slow, controlled manner.
  • Stretching: Hold each position for 10‑15 seconds. Never force the joint beyond its range.

Hydrotherapy (Underwater Treadmill)

The buoyancy of water reduces weight on joints while the resistance strengthens muscles. Ideal for dogs with arthritis, especially those with hip dysplasia or elbow arthritis. Many veterinary rehabilitation centers offer this service.

Balance and Strengthening Exercises

  • Balance discs: Standing on a stability disc strengthens core muscles and improves proprioception.
  • Weight shifting: Gently shifting weight from one leg to another improves balance and muscle strength.

3. Non‑Surgical Pain Management

  • NSAIDs (carprofen, meloxicam): Effective for reducing inflammation and pain. Require regular bloodwork monitoring. Never combine with human NSAIDs.
  • Adequan (polysulfated glycosaminoglycan): Injectable medication that stimulates cartilage repair and reduces inflammation. Typically given as a series of injections, then maintenance.
  • Librela (bedinvetmab): A monthly monoclonal antibody injection that targets nerve growth factor for pain relief without NSAID side effects. Approved for dogs in the US and EU.
  • Laser therapy: Low‑level laser therapy reduces inflammation and promotes tissue healing in arthritic joints. Available at many veterinary clinics.
  • Acupuncture: Can help reduce pain and inflammation in some pets. Works best in combination with other therapies.

4. Home Modifications for Arthritis

  • Ramps: For stairs, cars, and furniture – reduces strain on joints.
  • Orthopedic beds: Memory foam beds reduce pressure points and provide support.
  • Non‑slip flooring: Throw rugs or carpet runners prevent slipping on hard floors.
  • Elevated food and water bowls: Reduces neck and shoulder strain during feeding.

6. Your Action Plan

  1. Schedule a veterinary exam to confirm the diagnosis and discuss treatment options.
  2. Consider physical therapy (hydrotherapy, balance exercises) and non‑surgical pain management.
  3. Modify your home environment (ramps, orthopedic bed, non‑slip flooring).
  4. Monitor your pet's weight – even a 5‑10% weight loss can significantly improve arthritis symptoms.

Final thought: Arthritis is a progressive disease, but it's manageable. With physical therapy, non‑surgical pain relief, and lifestyle modifications, many pets can live comfortably for years. Don't wait – the earlier you start, the better the outcome.
