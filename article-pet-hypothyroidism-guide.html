📊 Prevalence: Hypothyroidism is one of the most common endocrine disorders in dogs, affecting approximately 1 in 200 dogs. It is rare in cats (most feline thyroid disorders are hyperthyroidism).

1. Early Warning Signs (Often Mistaken for Aging)

  • Weight gain – without increased appetite.
  • Lethargy and exercise intolerance – sleeping more, less interest in walks.
  • Hair loss and poor coat quality – thinning hair, dry skin, particularly on the trunk and tail (rat tail).
  • Cold intolerance – seeking warm spots, shivering.
  • Mental dullness – slower responses, disorientation.
  • Ear infections, skin infections – recurring issues.
⚠️ If your dog shows multiple signs, ask your vet for a thyroid panel – not just total T4, but also free T4 and TSH.

2. Why Diagnosis Can Be Tricky

Hypothyroidism shares symptoms with many other conditions (Cushing's disease, aging, diet). A single total T4 test can be falsely normal or low due to non‑thyroid illness (euthyroid sick syndrome). The gold standard is a **complete thyroid panel** including:

  • Total T4 (TT4) – initial screening.
  • Free T4 (fT4) by equilibrium dialysis – most accurate.
  • TSH (thyroid‑stimulating hormone) – elevated in primary hypothyroidism.

Your vet may also recommend a baseline and post‑stimulation TSH test for borderline cases.

3. Treatment: Levothyroxine (L‑thyroxine)

Hypothyroidism is managed with lifelong daily oral levothyroxine (Synthroid, ThyroKare). The dose is based on body weight and adjusted based on follow‑up bloodwork.

  • Starting dose: 0.02‑0.03 mg/kg twice daily (or once daily for some dogs).
  • Follow‑up testing: Recheck TT4 and free T4 4‑6 weeks after starting, then every 6‑12 months.
  • Side effects: Rare at correct doses; overdose can cause hyperactivity, panting, and weight loss (reversible with dose adjustment).

4. Dietary and Lifestyle Support

While no special diet cures hypothyroidism, maintaining a healthy weight is crucial. Use a lower‑calorie, high‑fiber food to prevent weight gain. Regular, gentle exercise helps manage weight and improve energy.

6. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Relying only on total T4 – can be falsely low due to other illnesses.
  • Giving the medication with high‑calcium foods – calcium can interfere with absorption.
  • Missing dose – give at the same time every day (preferably on an empty stomach, 1 hour before food).
  • Skipping rechecks – dose adjustments are common, especially after initial stabilization.

7. Your Action Plan

  1. If your dog shows multiple symptoms, ask your vet for a complete thyroid panel (TT4, free T4, TSH).
  2. Start levothyroxine as prescribed and monitor for improvement over 4‑6 weeks.
  3. Schedule recheck bloodwork to ensure the dose is correct.

Final thought: Hypothyroidism is one of the most treatable endocrine disorders in dogs. Lifelong medication costs approximately $20‑$40 per month. The improvement in quality of life is dramatic. Don't accept "old age" as an answer – ask your vet to test.
