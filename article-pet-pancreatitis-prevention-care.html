📊 Fact: Pancreatitis is one of the most common gastrointestinal emergencies in dogs and cats. Miniature Schnauzers, Yorkshire Terriers, and cats of any breed are at higher risk.

1. What Triggers Pancreatitis?

  • High‑fat meals – table scraps (turkey skin, bacon, gravy), fatty treats, or a sudden switch to high‑fat food.
  • Garbage ingestion – eating spoiled food or non‑food items.
  • Obesity – overweight pets are at higher risk.
  • Certain medications – some chemotherapy drugs, anticonvulsants (phenobarbital).
  • Idiopathic (unknown cause) – often in cats.
⚠️ Miniature Schnauzers have a genetic predisposition. If you own one, strict lifelong low‑fat diet is essential.

2. Early Signs (Don't Wait)

  • Vomiting – may be intermittent or severe.
  • Abdominal pain – hunched back, reluctance to move, crying when picked up.
  • Lethargy and decreased appetite – often the first sign in cats.
  • Diarrhea (possibly bloody) – common in dogs.
  • Fever and dehydration – signs of systemic inflammation.

3. Diagnosis & Immediate Treatment

Your vet will run bloodwork (elevated pancreatic lipase – cPL/fPL), possibly abdominal ultrasound. Mild cases may be treated at home with a bland diet (boiled chicken and rice) and anti‑nausea medication. Moderate to severe cases require hospitalization with IV fluids, pain control, and nutritional support.

4. Recovery Diet (Low‑Fat is Key)

After an episode, you must feed a low‑fat diet for at least 2‑4 weeks (or lifelong for recurrent cases). Fat content should be ≤ 10% dry matter (≤ 4‑5% for sensitive pets).

5. How to Prevent Future Episodes

  • No table scraps – even small amounts of fatty food can trigger relapse.
  • Feed a consistent low‑fat diet – don't switch brands abruptly.
  • Maintain a healthy weight – obesity increases risk.
  • Secure garbage cans – prevent scavenging.
  • For high‑risk breeds (Miniature Schnauzers) – lifelong low‑fat prescription diet.

7. Home‑Cooked Low‑Fat Recipe (Temporary Use Only)

If prescription food isn't available, a bland diet of 1 part boiled skinless chicken breast + 2 parts white rice can be used for 3‑5 days. Do not add salt, oil, or seasonings. After recovery, transition back to a complete and balanced commercial low‑fat diet.

8. Your Action Plan

  1. If you suspect pancreatitis, go to the vet immediately – don't wait.
  2. Ask about a cPL (dogs) or fPL (cats) snap test – results in 10 minutes.
  3. After recovery, feed a low‑fat diet and eliminate all table scraps.

Final thought: Pancreatitis can be fatal, but it's preventable. The cost of low‑fat prescription food ($50‑$80/month) is a fraction of the cost of an emergency hospitalization ($2,000‑$5,000). Keep fatty treats away from your pet, and if you have a high‑risk breed, commit to lifelong low‑fat feeding. Your pet's pancreas will thank you.
