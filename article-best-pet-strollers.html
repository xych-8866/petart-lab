1. Who Needs a Pet Stroller?

  • Senior dogs with arthritis or joint pain – can still enjoy walks without straining joints.
  • Small breeds (Chihuahua, Yorkie, Pomeranian) – get tired quickly and are vulnerable to larger dogs.
  • Cats – can safely experience the outdoors without risk of escaping or encountering predators.
  • Pets recovering from surgery – limited activity required, but mental stimulation still needed.
  • Travelers – lightweight, foldable strollers are great for airports, city trips, and farmers markets.

2. What to Look For

  • Weight capacity – most support 20‑50 lbs. Know your pet's weight.
  • Wheel type – 4‑wheel strollers are most stable; 3‑wheel strollers are more maneuverable for jogging.
  • Suspension & tires – rubber air‑filled tires provide the smoothest ride; plastic wheels are bumpier.
  • Foldability – one‑hand fold is ideal for travel and storage.
  • Safety features – interior tether to prevent jumping out, dual brakes, reflective trim.
  • Ventilation & visibility – mesh windows for airflow and for your pet to see out.

4. How to Choose the Right Size

  • Measure your pet's length (from nose to base of tail) and height (from floor to top of head when sitting).
  • Add 2‑3 inches to length and height – the pet should have room to turn around and lie down.
  • Check the stroller's interior dimensions (not just weight capacity). Some heavy strollers have narrow cabins.

6. Tips for Introducing Your Pet to a Stroller

  1. Leave the stroller open at home for a few days, let your pet sniff it and explore.
  2. Put treats and a familiar blanket inside. Reward your pet for stepping in.
  3. Take short, smooth rides around the house (no bumps).
  4. Gradually move to quiet outdoor areas, then to busier streets.
  5. Never leave your pet unattended in a stroller, especially in heat.

7. What to Avoid

  • Cheap plastic wheels – they break easily and provide a bumpy ride.
  • No interior tether – energetic pets can jump out and get injured.
  • Poor ventilation – can lead to overheating, especially for brachycephalic breeds.
  • Hard to fold – if you plan to travel, a one‑hand fold is essential.
