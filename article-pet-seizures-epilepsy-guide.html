📊 Prevalence: Epilepsy affects approximately 0.5‑5% of dogs, making it the most common neurological disorder in canines. Some breeds (German Shepherds, Border Collies, Golden Retrievers, Beagles) are more predisposed.

1. Types of Seizures

  • Generalized (tonic‑clonic): The classic "grand mal" – loss of consciousness, stiffening (tonic), paddling or jerking (clonic), salivation, loss of bladder/bowel control. Lasts 1‑3 minutes.
  • Focal (partial): Affects one part of the body – twitching of a leg, face, or repetitive movements. May progress to generalized.
  • Absence (petit mal): Brief periods of staring, unresponsiveness. Rare in dogs.
  • Cluster seizures: Multiple seizures in 24 hours – requires immediate emergency care.
  • Status epilepticus: Seizure lasting more than 5 minutes or multiple without recovery – a life‑threatening emergency.
⚠️ If a seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes or your pet has multiple in 24 hours, go to the emergency vet immediately.

2. What to Do During a Seizure

  • DO: Time the seizure (use your phone).
  • DO: Move objects away from your pet to prevent injury.
  • DO: Keep the environment dark and quiet (stimuli can prolong seizures).
  • DO: Stay calm and stay with your pet.
  • DO NOT: Put your hands near the mouth (they won't swallow their tongue, but you could get bitten).
  • DO NOT: Try to restrain or hold down your pet.
  • DO NOT: Put anything in the mouth.

3. Common Causes

  • Idiopathic epilepsy – no known cause; most common in young to middle‑aged dogs (1‑6 years).
  • Toxins – chocolate, xylitol, grapes, marijuana, snail bait, lead.
  • Metabolic disease – liver disease, kidney disease, hypoglycemia, electrolyte imbalance.
  • Infectious disease – distemper, tick‑borne diseases, fungal infections.
  • Head trauma – can cause brain damage leading to seizures.
  • Brain tumors – more common in older dogs (>7 years).

4. How Vets Diagnose Seizures

  • Complete bloodwork and urinalysis – to rule out metabolic causes.
  • Neurological exam – to assess brain function and localize lesions.
  • Bile acid test – to check liver function (hepatic encephalopathy can cause seizures).
  • MRI (brain scan) – recommended for older dogs or if structural disease is suspected.
  • Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) tap – to rule out inflammation or infection.

5. Treatment: Anti‑Epileptic Drugs (AEDs)

Treatment is started if your pet has more than one seizure per month, cluster seizures, or severe seizures. Medications are lifelong and require regular monitoring.

  • Phenobarbital – the most commonly used first‑line drug. Effective but requires monitoring of liver levels.
  • Levetiracetam (Keppra) – fewer side effects, often used as an add‑on therapy.
  • Potassium bromide – used alongside phenobarbital, especially in drug‑resistant cases.
  • Gabapentin – sometimes used for focal seizures or as an adjunct.

7. Monitoring and Long‑Term Management

  • Seizure diary – track date, time, duration, and what happened before.
  • Regular bloodwork – monitor drug levels and liver/kidney function.
  • Emergency plan – discuss rescue medications with your vet for home use.
  • Dosage adjustments – may be needed as your pet grows or ages.

8. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Stopping medication abruptly – can cause withdrawal seizures.
  • Skipping doses – inconsistent levels can lead to breakthrough seizures.
  • Not seeking emergency care for prolonged seizures – status epilepticus is a life‑threatening emergency.
  • Using CBD without veterinary guidance – some owners use CBD oil, but research is limited and quality varies widely.

9. Your Action Plan

  1. If your pet has a seizure, time it and stay calm. Call your vet.
  2. After the first seizure, schedule a full diagnostic workup with your vet.
  3. If epilepsy is diagnosed, work with your vet to find the right medication.
  4. Keep a seizure diary and never miss doses.

Final thought: A seizure diagnosis is not a death sentence. With proper medication and monitoring, most epileptic dogs live normal, happy lives. The medication cost is $20‑$50/month. The cost of emergency care for status epilepticus can exceed $2,000. Invest in veterinary care and medication – your pet's quality of life depends on it.
