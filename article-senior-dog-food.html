1. How Aging Changes Nutritional Needs

Senior dogs (typically age 7+ for large breeds, 9+ for small) undergo physiological shifts:

  • Reduced metabolic rate – 20‑30% lower than adults → prone to obesity if overfed.
  • Muscle loss (sarcopenia) – natural loss of 1‑2% muscle mass per year after age 8. Protein requirement actually increases to preserve muscle (min 25% dry matter, ideally 30‑35%).
  • Declining kidney function – ~30% of senior dogs have some kidney impairment. Need controlled phosphorus (below 0.6% dry matter) and high‑quality protein (not "low protein").
  • Dental issues – missing teeth or gum disease may make kibble painful.

Therefore, the ideal senior food is: high protein (30%+ dry matter), moderate fat (12‑15%), controlled phosphorus (under 0.6%), with added glucosamine/chondroitin.

4. Dry vs. Wet Food: Which Is Right for Your Senior?

Both have pros and cons. Many owners feed a mix.

  • Dry food – cheaper, convenient, helps scrape plaque (minimally). But low moisture (10%) can worsen dehydration. Choose small kibble size for senior teeth.
  • Wet food – high moisture (78%) helps kidneys and urinary tract. Easier to chew. But more expensive and less calorie‑dense (need larger volume).

Recommendation: Feed 75% high‑quality dry + 25% wet (or add warm water to dry). This balances cost, convenience, and hydration.

5. What to Avoid in Senior Dog Food

  • ❌ Corn, wheat, soy as top ingredients – empty calories, potential allergens.
  • ❌ Artificial colors, flavors, preservatives (BHA, BHT, ethoxyquin) – linked to health issues.
  • ❌ Vague meat sources ("meat meal", "animal digest") – low quality.
  • ❌ "Senior" formulas that are simply lower protein – muscle loss is a bigger risk than kidney disease for most dogs.

7. Your 3‑Step Action Plan

  1. Calculate daily calories – senior dogs need 20‑30% fewer calories than adults. Use an online calculator (e.g., Pet Nutrition Alliance).
  2. Transition over 7 days – mix old food with new (25% new day 1‑2, 50% day 3‑4, 75% day 5‑6, 100% day 7).
  3. Monitor – after 4 weeks, check body condition score (BCS). Adjust portions if overweight or underweight. Also watch for stool quality, energy level, and water intake.
