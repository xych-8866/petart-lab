📊 Prevalence: GDV (bloat) is one of the most life-threatening emergencies in dogs, with a mortality rate of 10-30% even with treatment. Large, deep-chested breeds (Great Danes, German Shepherds, Standard Poodles, Dobermans) are at highest risk.
🚨 EMERGENCY ACTION
If your dog is retching unproductively, has a distended abdomen, or is restless and drooling, go to the nearest emergency vet immediately. GDV is a life-threatening emergency – do not wait.

1. What Is GDV (Bloat)?

Gastric dilatation-volvulus (GDV) is a condition where the stomach fills with gas and twists on its axis (volvulus). This traps gas, stretches the stomach, and cuts off blood supply to the stomach and spleen. Without emergency treatment, the stomach can rupture, and the dog can go into shock and die within hours.

2. Breeds at Highest Risk

  • Large, deep-chested breeds: Great Danes, German Shepherds, Standard Poodles, Dobermans, Irish Wolfhounds, Weimaraners, St. Bernards
  • Older dogs (> 5 years): Risk increases with age
  • Dogs with a family history: GDV has a genetic component
  • Underweight dogs: May have more room for the stomach to rotate

3. Early Warning Signs (Don't Ignore)

  • Unproductive retching – trying to vomit but nothing comes up (most common sign)
  • Distended abdomen – stomach appears swollen and feels tight (like a drum)
  • Restlessness and pacing – unable to settle
  • Excessive drooling – more than usual
  • Rapid breathing – shallow panting
  • Pale gums – from reduced blood flow
⚠️ If you see these signs, especially unproductive retching and a swollen abdomen, go to the vet immediately. Do not try to induce vomiting or give antacids.

4. Diagnosis and Treatment

Your vet will quickly assess your dog, take X-rays to confirm the diagnosis, and prepare for emergency surgery. Treatment involves:

  • Stabilization: IV fluids, pain medication, and decompression (passing a tube to release gas)
  • Emergency surgery: Untwisting the stomach (derotation), checking for damaged tissue, and performing a gastropexy (attaching the stomach to the body wall to prevent recurrence)
  • Post-operative care: Monitoring for complications, pain management, and gradual re-introduction of food

5. Prevention Strategies

  • Feed multiple small meals – instead of one large meal
  • Slow down eating – use a slow feeder bowl or puzzle feeder
  • Avoid exercise after meals – wait at least 1 hour after eating before exercise
  • Keep water available – but avoid vigorous drinking after meals
  • Consider prophylactic gastropexy – preventive surgery for high-risk breeds
  • Manage stress – anxiety can contribute to GDV

7. What to Avoid

  • Exercising immediately after meals – wait at least 1 hour
  • Feeding from elevated bowls (for large breeds) – may increase risk
  • Free-feeding (leaving food out all day) – encourages overeating
  • Ignoring early signs – time is critical

8. Your Action Plan

  1. Know the signs of GDV – teach everyone in your household.
  2. If you have a high-risk breed, discuss prophylactic gastropexy with your vet.
  3. Feed multiple small meals and use a slow feeder bowl.
  4. Avoid exercise immediately after meals.

Final thought: GDV is a life-threatening emergency, but with rapid treatment, many dogs survive. Prevention is the best strategy: feeding multiple small meals, using slow feeders, and considering prophylactic gastropexy for high-risk breeds.
