1. Ear Infections (Otitis Externa)

Why it happens: Dogs with floppy ears (Cocker Spaniels, Retrievers) or narrow ear canals (Poodles) trap moisture, wax, and debris. Yeast (Malassezia) and bacteria (Staphylococcus, Pseudomonas) thrive in warm, damp environments. Allergies (food or environmental) are underlying causes in 60% of recurrent cases.

Early signs (before it smells):

  • Head shaking or tilting one ear down
  • Scratching at ears or rubbing face on carpet
  • Redness on inner ear flap or canal opening
  • Slight dark yellow or brown debris (normal is light pink with no debris)
⚠️ When to see the vet: foul smell, thick discharge, painful to touch, head tilt, or if your dog is lethargic. These indicate deep infection or possible ruptured eardrum.

Home prevention & mild case care:

Use a veterinarian‑approved ear cleanser weekly for floppy‑eared dogs. Never use cotton swabs (pushes debris deeper).

2. Hot Spots (Acute Moist Dermatitis)

Why it happens: A small itch (flea, allergy, matted hair) causes licking/chewing. Saliva destroys skin barriers, bacteria multiply, and within hours you have a raw, weeping, painful lesion. Hot spots can double in size in 8 hours.

Early signs:

  • Intense licking or biting at one spot
  • Moist, red, hairless patch – often on flank, tail base, or cheek
  • Warm to the touch (inflammation)

Home treatment (mild, small hot spots):

  1. Clip hair around the area with blunt‑tip scissors.
  2. Gently clean with chlorhexidine solution (dilute 1:10 with water).
  3. Apply a cooling spray or gel.
  4. Prevent licking – use an e‑collar (cone) for 48 hours.

3. Digestive Upsets (Vomiting & Diarrhea)

Why it happens: Dietary indiscretion (eating garbage, table scraps), stress, or sudden food changes. Most cases are self‑limiting.

Home care protocol for mild cases (no blood, no lethargy):

  • Withhold food for 12‑24 hours (but always provide water).
  • After fasting, feed a bland diet: 1 part boiled white rice + 1 part boiled boneless chicken/turkey (no skin, no oil). Feed small portions 4‑5 times a day.
  • Add probiotic – helps restore gut flora.
  • Gradually transition back to regular food over 2‑3 days.

4. Dental Disease (Gingivitis & Periodontitis)

Why it happens: Plaque hardens into tartar within 48 hours. Bacteria under the gumline destroys bone and ligaments, leading to loose teeth, pain, and bacteria entering the bloodstream (damaging heart, liver, kidneys). 80% of dogs have active dental disease by age 3.

Early signs (before bad breath):

  • Light brown line along gumline (plaque)
  • Pink or red gums instead of pale pink
  • Occasional pawing at mouth

Home prevention:

  • Daily brushing is gold standard. Use dog toothpaste (never human).
  • Dental chews – look for VOHC (Veterinary Oral Health Council) seal.
  • Water additives – help reduce plaque but don't replace brushing.

5. Daily 5‑Minute Health Check (Do This Every Evening)

Head & ears: Look for redness, debris, or odor.
Eyes: Clear, no discharge.
Mouth: Lift lips – check for tartar, red gums.
Skin & coat: Run your hand over body – feel for lumps, scabs, or wet spots.
Feet & nails: Check between paw pads for cuts or burrs.
Overall behavior: Is your dog eating, drinking, peeing, pooping normally? Any vomiting, diarrhea, coughing, sneezing?
