📊 Prevalence: Snoring affects up to 50% of brachycephalic (flat‑faced) dogs, including Bulldogs, Pugs, and Boston Terriers. Sleep apnea – where breathing repeatedly stops during sleep – is a serious condition that can lead to heart problems and reduced quality of life.

1. Normal Snoring vs. Sleep Apnea

Normal SnoringSleep Apnea
Soft, rhythmic, consistentLoud, irregular, with pauses
Occurs in certain sleep positionsOccurs in all positions
No gasping or choking soundsGasping, choking, or snorting sounds
Pet wakes up rested and alertPet wakes up groggy, tired, or restless
No daytime sleepinessExcessive daytime sleepiness
⚠️ If your pet gasps, chokes, or stops breathing during sleep, or is excessively sleepy during the day, see your vet promptly.

2. Common Causes of Snoring and Sleep Apnea

  • Brachycephalic airway syndrome – flat‑faced breeds with narrowed nostrils, elongated soft palate, and small tracheas.
  • Obesity – excess fat around the neck and throat narrows the airway.
  • Allergies – inflammation in the nasal passages from environmental or food allergies.
  • Polyps or tumors – growths in the nasal passage or throat.
  • Sleep position – some dogs snore when sleeping on their back.

3. Breeds at Highest Risk

  • Dogs: Bulldogs, Pugs, French Bulldogs, Boston Terriers, Shih Tzus, Pekingese, Boxers
  • Cats: Persians, Himalayans, Exotic Shorthairs
  • Obesity increases the risk in any breed, regardless of face shape.

4. Health Risks of Sleep Apnea

  • Heart disease – repeated oxygen deprivation strains the heart.
  • High blood pressure – from chronic stress on the cardiovascular system.
  • Poor sleep quality – leads to daytime sleepiness and reduced quality of life.
  • Increased risk of heatstroke – due to compromised breathing.
  • Shortened lifespan – severe, untreated apnea can reduce life expectancy.

5. Diagnosis and Treatment

  • Veterinary exam – your vet will examine the airway, check for polyps or tumors, and assess the soft palate.
  • Imaging – X‑rays or CT scans may be used to evaluate the airway structure.
  • Weight management – even a 5‑10% weight loss can significantly improve breathing.
  • Brachycephalic airway surgery – widening nostrils (stent), trimming the soft palate, and removing excess tissue.
  • Allergy management – antihistamines or dietary changes to reduce inflammation.
  • Elevated sleeping position – using a raised bed or pillow can help keep the airway open.

7. What to Avoid

  • Ignoring loud or irregular snoring – it often indicates a breathing problem.
  • Overfeeding brachycephalic breeds – obesity worsens breathing issues.
  • Using sedatives or sleeping aids – can relax the airway muscles and worsen apnea.
  • Excessive exercise in hot weather – dogs with compromised airways are at higher risk of heatstroke.

8. Your Action Plan

  1. Monitor your pet's sleeping patterns – is snoring occasional or consistent?
  2. Look for signs of apnea: gasping, choking, or pauses in breathing.
  3. If you suspect sleep apnea, schedule a veterinary exam.
  4. Maintain a healthy weight and consider an elevated bed to improve airway position.

Final thought: Snoring is not always harmless, especially in flat‑faced breeds. If your pet's snoring is loud, irregular, or accompanied by gasping, seek veterinary care. Early intervention can improve your pet's quality of life and prevent serious health complications.
