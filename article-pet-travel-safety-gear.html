1. Types of Pet Car Restraints (And Why Most "Car Harnesses" Are Junk)

There are three main types, but only one category has been independently crash‑tested to a recognized standard.

  • Seat belt tethers (clip‑to‑harness) – attaches to harness on pet, clips into seatbelt buckle. Best option if the harness is crash‑tested.
  • Car crates / travel kennels – must be strapped down and tested for impact. Heavy but safest for small pets.
  • Car barriers / back seat hammocks – prevent forward motion but do not restrain the pet; the pet still becomes a projectile in a side impact.
⚠️ Warning: The Center for Pet Safety (CPS) conducts independent crash testing. Look for their "CPS Certified" seal – not just "tested" or "recommended." Many Amazon products claim safety with no third‑party validation.

5. How to Prevent Car Sickness & Anxiety

  • Start with short drives – just to the mailbox, then around the block.
  • Use a pheromone spray (Adaptil, Feliway) – spray on crate blanket 15 minutes before departure.
  • Travel on an empty stomach – feed 3‑4 hours before driving to reduce nausea.
  • Ginger or motion sickness medication – ask your vet for safe options (meclizine, Cerenia).
  • Never open windows fully – debris can injure eyes; dogs can jump out.

7. What the Law Says

Only a few states (Rhode Island, New Jersey, California) have specific laws about restraining pets in vehicles, but even where not required, you can be charged with distracted driving if an unrestrained pet interferes. Many insurance companies will deny injury claims if an unrestrained pet contributed to the crash.
