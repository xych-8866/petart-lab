Pet Urinary Tract Infections: Symptoms, Causes & Treatment (2026)

What You'll Learn

  • Early signs: frequent urination, straining, blood in urine, accidents
  • Common causes (bacteria, bladder stones, diabetes, stress)
  • Diagnosis: urinalysis, culture, and imaging
  • Treatment: antibiotics, dietary changes, and supplements
  • How to prevent recurrent UTIs in dogs and cats

When 4‑year‑old cat, Luna, started urinating outside her litter box and crying when she went, her owner assumed it was behavioral. But after a vet visit, Luna was diagnosed with a severe urinary tract infection (UTI). A course of antibiotics and a prescription diet cleared the infection within two weeks. "I had no idea UTIs were so painful and could cause litter box avoidance," her owner says.

📊 Prevalence: UTIs are common in dogs and cats, affecting up to 15-20% of pets at some point. Female dogs are more prone due to shorter urethras. In cats, stress and struvite crystals are common contributors.

1. Early Warning Signs

  • Frequent urination – asking to go out more often, small amounts each time.
  • Straining to urinate – posture changes, vocalizing, difficulty.
  • Blood in urine – pink or red tinge.
  • Accidents in the house – previously house‑trained pets may have accidents.
  • Licking the genital area – excessive grooming.
  • Lethargy, fever, loss of appetite – signs of systemic infection.
⚠️ If your pet is unable to urinate at all, this is a medical emergency – seek immediate veterinary care (urethral obstruction can be fatal).

2. Causes of UTIs

  • Bacteria – the most common cause, often from fecal contamination or environmental exposure.
  • Bladder stones (struvite, oxalate) – irritate the bladder lining and promote infection.
  • Diabetes mellitus – high sugar in urine encourages bacterial growth.
  • Stress and anxiety – especially in cats, stress can contribute to idiopathic cystitis.
  • Anatomical issues – female dogs have shorter urethras, making them more susceptible.

3. Diagnosis

Your vet will collect a urine sample (via cystocentesis, catheter, or free catch) for:

  • Urinalysis – checks for bacteria, crystals, blood, and pH.
  • Urine culture and sensitivity – identifies the specific bacteria and which antibiotics will be effective.
  • Imaging (X‑rays or ultrasound) – to check for bladder stones or structural abnormalities.

4. Treatment Options

  • Antibiotics – based on culture results; typically given for 7‑14 days. Always finish the full course.
  • Pain management – anti‑inflammatory drugs may be prescribed for discomfort.
  • Dietary changes – prescription urinary diets help dissolve crystals and prevent recurrence (e.g., Hill's c/d, Royal Canin Urinary SO).
  • Increased water intake – dilute urine reduces bacterial growth; use water fountains and wet food.

5. Preventing Recurrent UTIs

  • Provide fresh, clean water daily – consider a water fountain.
  • Feed a urinary health diet – if your pet is prone to crystals or stones.
  • Give your pet frequent potty breaks – don't hold urine for too long.
  • Reduce stress – for cats, provide perches, hiding spots, and pheromone diffusers (Feliway).
  • Maintain good hygiene – keep the genital area clean, especially for long‑haired dogs.

6. Real Owner Success Story

"My 7‑year‑old female Beagle, Daisy, had recurrent UTIs every few months. We tried antibiotics but they kept coming back. After switching to a prescription urinary diet and adding a water fountain, Daisy hasn't had a UTI in over a year. It's been life‑changing." – Sarah, California

7. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Stopping antibiotics too early – can lead to resistance and recurrence.
  • Ignoring underlying causes – such as bladder stones or diabetes.
  • Using human pain relievers – some are toxic to pets.
  • Not increasing water intake – dehydration concentrates urine and promotes bacterial growth.

8. Your Action Plan

  1. If you notice signs of a UTI, collect a urine sample (if possible) and call your vet.
  2. Follow the prescribed antibiotic course completely.
  3. Implement preventive measures (diet, water, stress reduction) to avoid recurrence.

Final thought: UTIs are painful and can lead to serious complications if untreated. Early recognition and proper treatment are key. Investing in a prescription urinary diet and encouraging water intake can significantly reduce recurrence and improve your pet's quality of life.
