1. How to Safely Remove a Tick

DO NOT: Use fingernails, burning the tick, or twisting. These methods can leave mouthparts embedded, increasing infection risk.

DO: Use fine‑tipped tweezers or a tick remover tool.

  1. Grasp the tick as close to the skin as possible.
  2. Pull straight upward with steady, even pressure (do not twist).
  3. Clean the bite area with rubbing alcohol or soap and water.
  4. Save the tick in a sealed container (in case of symptoms, your vet may test it).

2. Signs of Flea Infestation

  • Excessive scratching, biting, or licking – especially around the tail base, groin, and neck.
  • Flea dirt (black specks) – looks like black pepper; dissolves red on a wet paper towel (it's dried blood).
  • Hair loss or scabs – from allergic reactions to flea saliva.
  • Tapeworms – fleas carry tapeworm eggs; if you see rice‑like segments in stool, your pet likely has fleas.

4. Treating Your Home for Fleas

If your pet has fleas, you must treat the environment (home, yard) as well. Fleas lay eggs that fall off your pet and hatch in carpets and furniture.

  • Wash all pet bedding in hot water.
  • Vacuum carpets, sofas, and floors daily for at least a week.
  • Use an environmental flea spray (e.g., Vet's Best Flea Spray) on carpets and furniture.
  • For severe infestations, consider professional pest control or foggers.

5. Natural Remedies (Use With Caution)

  • Cedar oil, lemon spray, or diatomaceous earth – may repel or kill fleas, but are less effective than veterinary products.
  • Essential oils (e.g., lavender, peppermint) – can be toxic to cats and some dogs. Use only veterinary‑approved formulas.
  • Veterinary products are proven to work and are worth the cost for effective protection.

6. Diseases Transmitted by Fleas and Ticks

  • Lyme disease – causes fever, lameness, kidney damage. Treatable with antibiotics.
  • Ehrlichiosis – tick‑borne bacterial infection affecting blood cells.
  • Anaplasmosis – causes fever, joint pain, and lethargy.
  • Tapeworms – fleas carry tapeworm eggs; pets swallow fleas while grooming.
  • Bartonella (cat scratch disease) – transmitted by fleas to cats.
