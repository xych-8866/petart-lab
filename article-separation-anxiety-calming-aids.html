1. Is It Separation Anxiety or Just Boredom?

True separation anxiety occurs only when the pet is left alone – not when you're present. Key signs include:

  • Destructive behavior – focused on exit points (doors, windows, owner's belongings).
  • Vocalization – howling, barking, whining within minutes of departure.
  • House soiling – urinating/defecating even if house‑trained.
  • Panting, drooling, pacing – signs of physiological distress.
  • Self‑injury – licking/chewing paws or tail until raw.
💡 Boredom vs. anxiety: Bored dogs may destroy items but usually not the same exit points, and they often settle after the first 20 minutes. Anxious dogs escalate and rarely rest.

2. The Desensitization Protocol (Gold Standard Treatment)

Never leave your anxious pet alone during training – use daycare, a pet sitter, or take time off work. This 6‑week plan requires 10‑15 minutes daily.

  • Week 1: Pick up keys, put on coat, sit down – do NOT leave. Repeat 10‑15 times/day. Goal: disassociate triggers from departure.
  • Week 2: Open door, step outside, immediately return. Gradually increase time outside (1 second, 2 seconds, 5 seconds).
  • Week 3: Stay outside for 10‑30 seconds. Return before pet shows distress.
  • Week 4: Leave for 1‑2 minutes. Use a pet camera to monitor. If pet stays calm, extend.
  • Week 5: 5‑10 minute absences.
  • Week 6: 30‑60 minutes.

If your pet reacts, you moved too fast. Go back two steps. Consistency and patience are key.

5. When to Consider Veterinary Medication

If your pet still can't settle after 8‑12 weeks of consistent training and calming products, ask your vet about:

  • Fluoxetine (Prozac®) – SSRI, long‑term daily use. Effective for moderate‑severe anxiety.
  • Trazodone – short‑acting, given 1‑2 hours before expected departure.
  • Gabapentin – reduces anxiety and pain, used alone or with trazodone.
⚠️ Never use human anxiety medications without veterinary supervision. Some (like Xanax) can cause paradoxical excitation in dogs.

6. What NOT to Do

  • Don't punish – scolding increases anxiety and can lead to aggression.
  • Don't use bark collars – aversives make anxiety worse and can cause physical harm.
  • Don't get a second pet – unless the anxiety is due to isolation (not separation from a specific person), a second animal often doesn't help and can double the problem.
