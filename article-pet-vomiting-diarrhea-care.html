📊 Prevalence: Digestive upset is one of the most common reasons for veterinary visits in both dogs and cats, accounting for up to 20% of all visits.

1. Common Causes of Vomiting & Diarrhea

  • Dietary indiscretion – eating garbage, table scraps, or spoiled food.
  • Sudden diet change – switching food without transitioning.
  • Food allergies or intolerances – common proteins (chicken, beef) or grains.
  • Parasites – worms, Giardia, Tritrichomonas (especially in cats).
  • Bacterial or viral infections – salmonella, E. coli, parvovirus, feline panleukopenia.
  • Toxins – chocolate, xylitol, grapes, onions, plants (lilies, sago palm).
  • Stress – travel, boarding, new pets, or environmental changes.
  • Underlying disease – kidney disease, liver disease, pancreatitis, IBD, hyperthyroidism.
⚠️ If your pet has persistent vomiting or diarrhea (more than 24 hours), is lethargic, or has blood in stool/vomit, seek vet care immediately.

2. When to Treat at Home vs. When to See the Vet

Treat at home if:

  • Pet is otherwise bright, alert, and active.
  • Vomiting/Diarrhea is mild (1‑2 episodes).
  • No blood in vomit or stool.
  • No other symptoms (fever, lethargy, abdominal pain).

See vet immediately if:

  • Vomiting/Diarrhea persists for more than 24 hours.
  • Blood in vomit or stool (red or dark/black).
  • Lethargy, weakness, or collapse.
  • Abdominal pain (whining, hunched back).
  • Fever (over 103°F).
  • Suspected toxin ingestion or foreign body.
  • If your pet is very young, very old, or has pre‑existing health conditions.

3. Home Care Protocol

Step 1: Withhold food for 12‑24 hours
For adult dogs and cats (not for puppies, kittens, or senior/sick pets). Provide fresh water at all times.
Step 2: Bland diet
After fasting, feed a bland diet: boiled chicken (skinless) + white rice (plain, no salt/oil). Ratio: 1 part chicken to 2 parts rice. Offer small portions 4‑5 times daily.
Step 3: Probiotics
Add a vet‑recommended probiotic to restore gut flora (e.g., FortiFlora, Proviable).
Step 4: Gradual transition
Over 2‑3 days, slowly mix in their regular food, increasing the ratio while reducing the bland diet.

6. What to Avoid

  • Human anti‑diarrheal medication (e.g., Imodium, Pepto‑Bismol) – can be toxic to pets.
  • Dairy products (milk, cheese, yogurt) – can worsen diarrhea.
  • High‑fat foods – can trigger pancreatitis.
  • Force‑feeding – can cause aspiration pneumonia.

7. Your Action Plan

  1. Assess severity: mild (treat at home) vs. severe (see vet).
  2. If mild, implement the home care protocol (fasting, bland diet, probiotics).
  3. If no improvement within 24‑48 hours, see your vet.
  4. Prevent future episodes by avoiding table scraps and transitioning diets slowly.

Final thought: Most cases of vomiting and diarrhea resolve with supportive care. The cost of probiotics and bland diet food is minimal compared to emergency vet visits. However, always err on the side of caution – if your pet seems unwell, seek veterinary advice promptly.
