When 5‑year‑old cat, Oliver, started having chronic diarrhea and vomiting, his owner tried multiple over‑the‑counter diets. Nothing worked. After months of weight loss and a poor coat, a veterinary gastroenterologist diagnosed Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) – a condition where the intestinal lining becomes thickened and inflamed, impairing nutrient absorption. "Oliver is now stable and thriving."

📊 Prevalence: Chronic gastrointestinal disease affects up to 10‑15% of dogs and cats. IBD is one of the most common causes of chronic vomiting and diarrhea in cats and dogs.

1. Early Signs (Often Overlooked)

  • Chronic vomiting (more than 2‑3 times per month)
  • Chronic diarrhea (loose stools, increased frequency)
  • Weight loss despite normal appetite
  • Poor coat quality (dull, dry, excessive shedding)
  • Flatulence and abdominal rumbling
  • Loss of appetite (in some cases)
⚠️ If your pet has persistent digestive issues, don't just switch diets – see your vet for a proper workup.

2. Causes of Chronic Digestive Issues

  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) – immune‑mediated inflammation of the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Food allergies or intolerances – often to common proteins (chicken, beef, dairy) or grains.
  • Parasites – worms, Giardia, Tritrichomonas (especially in cats).
  • Exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (EPI) – lack of digestive enzymes.
  • Bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) – imbalance of gut flora.
  • Stress – can exacerbate underlying GI conditions, especially in cats.

3. How Vets Diagnose GI Issues

  • Fecal tests – to rule out parasites.
  • Bloodwork (complete blood count, biochemistry, thyroid, pancreatic lipase) – to check for systemic disease.
  • Abdominal ultrasound – to assess intestinal wall thickness, lymph nodes, and rule out tumors.
  • GI panel (TLI, B12, folate) – to detect EPI or B12 deficiency.
  • Intestinal biopsy (endoscopy or surgical) – the gold standard for IBD diagnosis.

4. Treatment Options

  • Dietary management – prescription hydrolyzed protein diets or novel protein diets (duck, rabbit, venison).
  • Probiotics and prebiotics – to restore healthy gut flora.
  • B12 supplementation – many pets with chronic GI disease are B12 deficient.
  • Immunosuppressive medication – steroids (prednisolone) or other immunomodulators (chlorambucil, cyclosporine) for moderate to severe IBD.
  • Anti‑emetics and anti‑diarrheals – for symptom control.

6. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Switching diets too quickly – may worsen symptoms.
  • Relying on over‑the‑counter probiotics without veterinary guidance – many are not clinically proven.
  • Stopping medications prematurely – IBD is often a lifelong condition requiring maintenance therapy.
  • Not treating underlying anxiety – stress can trigger GI flare‑ups in cats.

7. Your Action Plan

  1. Schedule a veterinary appointment for a comprehensive GI workup.
  2. Ask for a GI panel (TLI, B12, folate) and abdominal ultrasound.
  3. Implement a hydrolyzed or novel protein diet trial for 8‑12 weeks.
  4. Work with your vet to taper medications to the lowest effective dose.

Final thought: Chronic digestive issues are frustrating for both pets and owners, but they are manageable with proper diagnosis. The cost of an initial GI workup ($400‑$800) is far less than the cost of repeated emergency visits and chronic malnutrition. Don't give up – most pets with IBD can live long, comfortable lives with the right treatment.
