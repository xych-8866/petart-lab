When 6-year-old Pug, Gus, started squinting and pawing at his eye, his owner noticed a cloudy spot on his cornea. A veterinary ophthalmologist diagnosed a corneal ulcer – a painful erosion of the eye's surface. With prompt treatment, Gus's eye healed, but his owner learned a valuable lesson: eye problems can escalate quickly and lead to blindness if ignored.

📊 Prevalence: Eye diseases are among the most common reasons for veterinary visits in dogs and cats. Brachycephalic breeds (Pugs, Bulldogs, Shih Tzus) are at higher risk due to their prominent eyes and shallow eye sockets.

1. Conjunctivitis (Red Eye / Pink Eye)

Conjunctivitis is inflammation of the conjunctiva – the thin membrane lining the eyelids and covering the white part of the eye.

Signs:

  • Redness of the eye
  • Discharge (clear, yellow, or green)
  • Squinting or excessive blinking
  • Pawing at the eye

Causes: Allergies, infections (bacterial or viral), foreign bodies, dry eye.

Treatment: Antibiotic or anti-inflammatory eye drops, artificial tears, or allergy medication.

2. Corneal Ulcers

A corneal ulcer is an open sore on the cornea (the clear front surface of the eye). It can be caused by trauma, foreign bodies, or underlying conditions.

Signs:

  • Squinting or holding the eye closed
  • Cloudy appearance on the eye's surface
  • Redness and discharge
  • Pain (crying, pawing, rubbing)
  • Sensitivity to light
⚠️ Corneal ulcers are painful and can lead to blindness or perforation if untreated. Seek veterinary care immediately if you suspect an ulcer.

Treatment: Antibiotic drops, pain medication, and sometimes surgery (in severe cases).

3. Glaucoma

Glaucoma is an increase in intraocular pressure (IOP) that damages the optic nerve. It can be primary (genetic) or secondary (caused by other eye diseases).

Signs:

  • Redness in the eye
  • Cloudy cornea
  • Dilated pupil that does not respond to light
  • Pain (squinting, rubbing)
  • Enlarged eyeball (in chronic cases)
  • Loss of vision

Treatment: Pressure-lowering eye drops, oral medications, and in some cases, surgery (laser or drainage implants).

5. When to See an Emergency Vet

  • Your pet is squinting and holding the eye closed
  • There is a visible cloudiness or blue/grey haze on the eye
  • You notice a bulging or enlarged eye
  • You see a foreign body (grass seed, splinter)
  • Your pet is in severe pain

6. Prevention and Home Care

  • Regular eye cleaning – use pet-safe eye wipes to remove debris
  • Monitor for discharge – early intervention prevents progression
  • Protect eyes during outdoor activities – use protective goggles for high-risk breeds
  • Keep grooming products away from eyes – shampoos and sprays can cause irritation

7. Real Owner Success Story

"My 4-year-old French Bulldog, Leo, developed a corneal ulcer after a minor scratch. I noticed him squinting and took him to the vet immediately. With antibiotic drops and pain medication, he healed within a week. I learned that prompt treatment is key to saving vision." – Jessica, Florida

8. Your Action Plan

  1. Monitor your pet's eyes daily for redness, discharge, or squinting
  2. If you notice any signs, schedule a vet appointment promptly
  3. For severe signs (pain, cloudiness, bulging), go to an emergency vet
  4. Keep an eye care kit at home (wipes, artificial tears)
  5. Protect high-risk breeds (brachycephalic) from trauma

Final thought: Eye diseases are common but treatable when caught early. The cost of eye medications ($30-$100) and veterinary exams is far less than the cost of vision loss or surgical intervention. Watch your pet's eyes closely – early detection saves sight.
