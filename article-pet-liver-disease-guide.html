📊 Prevalence: Liver disease is one of the most common causes of death in older cats and dogs. Early diagnosis dramatically improves outcomes.

1. Early Warning Signs

  • Jaundice (yellowing of eyes, gums, skin) – a hallmark sign.
  • Vomiting and diarrhea – persistent digestive upset.
  • Lethargy and weakness – reduced energy, sleeping more.
  • Loss of appetite – often pronounced.
  • Increased thirst and urination – liver disease can affect kidney function.
  • Abdominal swelling (ascites) – fluid accumulation in the abdomen.
  • Behavioral changes (hepatic encephalopathy) – confusion, pacing, circling, head pressing.
⚠️ Yellow gums or eyes are a medical emergency. See your vet immediately.

3. How Vets Diagnose Liver Disease

  • Bloodwork (complete blood count, biochemistry) – elevated liver enzymes (ALT, AST, ALP, GGT) and bilirubin.
  • Bile acids test – measures liver function (fasting and post‑meal).
  • Abdominal ultrasound – visualizes liver size, texture, bile ducts, and any masses.
  • Liver biopsy – the gold standard for definitive diagnosis (fine needle aspirate or surgical biopsy).

4. Treatment Options

  • Dietary management – low‑copper, high‑quality protein, easily digestible diets (e.g., Hill's l/d, Royal Canin Hepatic).
  • Liver support supplements (Denamarin, SAMe, milk thistle) – protect liver cells and support regeneration.
  • Antibiotics – for bacterial infections (e.g., biliary tract infections).
  • Ursodiol (ursodeoxycholic acid) – helps thin bile and improve liver function.
  • Anti‑emetics – to control vomiting.
  • IV fluids and nutritional support – for acute, severe cases or anorexia.
  • Surgery – for portosystemic shunt correction or removal of masses.

6. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Ignoring early signs (vomiting, weight loss, jaundice) – early treatment prevents progression.
  • Giving NSAIDs without veterinary guidance – some NSAIDs are hepatotoxic.
  • Using supplements without veterinary approval – some can interfere with medication.
  • Stopping liver support medication prematurely – liver disease is often chronic and requires ongoing management.

7. Your Action Plan

  1. If you notice any signs of liver disease, schedule a vet appointment immediately.
  2. Follow your vet's diagnostic recommendations (bloodwork, ultrasound, biopsy).
  3. Start liver support supplements (Denamarin) as directed and feed a prescription hepatic diet.
  4. Schedule regular recheck bloodwork to monitor liver values.

Final thought: Liver disease is manageable with early diagnosis and proper treatment. The cost of liver support supplements ($30‑$60/month) and prescription diets is a small price compared to the cost of liver failure (hospitalization, ICU care). Monitor your pet's appetite, energy, and gum color – and act quickly if something feels off.
