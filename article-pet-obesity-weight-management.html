1. Is Your Pet Overweight? (Body Condition Score)

  • Ideal (4‑5/9): Ribs easily felt with slight fat covering; visible waist behind ribs; abdominal tuck.
  • Overweight (6‑7/9): Ribs difficult to feel; no waist; no abdominal tuck; fat deposits over spine.
  • Obese (8‑9/9): Ribs cannot be felt; heavy fat deposits; abdomen distended.

2. How Many Calories Does Your Pet Need?

Resting energy requirement (RER) formula: 70 x (body weight in kg)^0.75. For weight loss, multiply by 0.8 (20% calorie reduction). Example: A 50‑lb dog (22.7 kg) needs about 700 calories/day for maintenance; for weight loss, about 560 calories/day.

Use a kitchen scale to measure food – not a cup. A "cup" can vary by 20‑30%.

4. Portion Control & Treats

  • Use a measuring cup or kitchen scale – never "eyeball" portions.
  • Limit treats to 10% of daily calories – use low‑calorie treats (3‑5 calories each).
  • Replace high‑calorie treats with vegetables – green beans, carrots, cucumbers (for dogs; cats are picky).
  • Slow feeder bowls – help pets eat more slowly and feel fuller.

5. Exercise Tips for Overweight Pets

  • Start slow: 5‑10 minute walks, twice daily. Gradually increase.
  • Swimming – low‑impact, great for arthritic overweight dogs.
  • Indoor play – laser pointer for cats, fetch for dogs, puzzle toys.

7. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Cutting calories too drastically – can cause nutrient deficiencies and muscle loss. Reduce by 20‑30% max.
  • Not measuring treats – a single high‑calorie treat can undo a day's effort.
  • Free‑feeding (leaving food out all day) – makes portion control impossible.
  • Giving human food – even small amounts add calories (a single slice of cheese = 100 calories!).

8. Your 4‑Week Weight Loss Plan

  1. Week 1: Calculate current daily calories. Switch to weight management food over 5‑7 days.
  2. Week 2: Reduce daily calories by 20%. Use a kitchen scale to measure every meal.
  3. Week 3: Add 5‑minute daily walks (or play sessions).
  4. Week 4: Weigh your pet. Target 1‑2% body weight loss per week for dogs, 0.5‑1% for cats. Adjust portions as needed.
