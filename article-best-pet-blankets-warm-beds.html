1. Which Pets Need Extra Warmth?

  • Small dogs (under 15 lbs) – lose heat quickly due to high surface‑to‑volume ratio.
  • Short‑haired breeds – lack insulating undercoat.
  • Senior pets – reduced metabolism and muscle mass.
  • Pets with arthritis – warmth soothes joint pain.
  • Pets in cold climates – during winter months.

2. What to Look For

  • Soft, insulating materials – fleece, microfiber, sherpa, or faux fur.
  • Machine washable – essential for maintaining cleanliness.
  • Non‑slip backing – prevents the bed from sliding on hard floors.
  • Size appropriate – the pet should be able to stretch out or curl up comfortably.
  • Self‑warming or heated options – self‑warming beds reflect pet's body heat; heated beds require electricity.

4. How to Introduce a New Bed or Blanket

  1. Place the new bed/blanket in your pet's favorite resting spot.
  2. Add an old t‑shirt or blanket with your scent to make it familiar.
  3. Encourage your pet with treats and praise when they investigate.
  4. If using a heated bed, supervise initially to ensure your pet doesn't chew the cord.

5. Safety Tips for Heated Beds

  • Check for chew‑proof cords – especially if your pet chews things.
  • Never use with incontinent pets – moisture can damage the heating element.
  • Choose low‑wattage – designed for continuous use without overheating.
  • Always follow manufacturer instructions – don't cover the cord or use extension cords.

7. What to Avoid

  • Thin, cheap blankets – don't provide enough insulation.
  • Heated beds without safety features – look for automatic shut‑off or low‑voltage designs.
  • Non‑machine washable covers – can harbor bacteria and odors.
  • Blankets with loose threads – can be swallowed or cause entanglement.
