📊 Prevalence: Bladder stones affect approximately 1‑2% of dogs and 2‑5% of cats. Struvite stones are the most common, especially in cats, while calcium oxalate stones are increasingly common in both species.

1. What Are Bladder Stones and Crystals?

Bladder stones (uroliths) are mineral deposits that form in the urinary bladder. Crystals are microscopic mineral formations that often precede stone formation. Stones can cause irritation, infection, and even life‑threatening urethral obstruction (especially in male cats).

3. Signs of Bladder Stones

  • Blood in urine – often the first sign (pink or red discoloration).
  • Straining to urinate – frequent attempts with little urine output.
  • Accidents in the house – especially in previously house‑trained pets.
  • Licking genital area – sign of discomfort.
  • Vocalizing while urinating – sign of pain.
⚠️ If your male cat is unable to urinate (obstruction), this is a life‑threatening emergency – go to the nearest emergency vet immediately.

4. Diagnosis

  • Urinalysis – detects blood, crystals, and pH.
  • Urine culture – to check for infection.
  • X‑rays (radiographs) – many stones are visible on X‑ray.
  • Ultrasound – useful for detecting stones not visible on X‑ray.
  • Stone analysis – if stones are removed, they should be analyzed for type to guide prevention.

5. Treatment Options

  • Prescription diets – for struvite stones, special diets can dissolve them (e.g., Hill's c/d, Royal Canin Urinary SO).
  • Surgical removal (cystotomy) – for stones that are large, not dissolvable, or causing obstruction.
  • Lithotripsy – non‑surgical shock wave therapy (less common).
  • Antibiotics – if a UTI is present.
  • Pain management – for comfort during treatment.

6. Prevention

  • Increase water intake – the most important preventive step. Use water fountains, wet food, and multiple water stations.
  • Feed a prescription urinary diet – for pets prone to stones.
  • Regular urinalysis – monitor for crystals and pH changes.
  • Maintain a healthy weight – obesity increases risk.
  • Provide frequent opportunities to urinate – don't hold urine for too long.

8. Your Action Plan

  1. If you notice blood in urine or straining, see your vet immediately.
  2. Follow your vet's recommendations (diet, water intake, medication).
  3. For pets prone to stones, feed a prescription urinary diet long‑term.
  4. Monitor water intake and urine color regularly.
