📊 Prevalence: Allergies affect up to 20‑30% of dogs and 10‑15% of cats. Environmental allergies (atopy) are the most common type, but food allergies account for approximately 10‑15% of cases.

2. Environmental Allergies (Atopic Dermatitis)

Signs: Itching localized to paws, face, ears, armpits, and groin. Recurrent ear infections, licking, and chewing are common. Often seasonal initially but can progress to year‑round.

Management:

  • Reduce allergen exposure – wash bedding, use air purifiers, wipe paws after walks.
  • Antihistamines – may provide mild relief; often used in combination with other treatments.
  • Immunotherapy (allergy shots) – the only treatment that can modify the underlying allergy. High success rate (70‑80%).
  • Topical therapy – medicated shampoos, ear cleaners, and soothing sprays.

3. Food Allergies

Signs: Itching (often generalized), recurrent ear infections, gastrointestinal signs (vomiting, diarrhea, flatulence). Unlike environmental allergies, food allergies don't fluctuate with seasons.

Diagnosis: An elimination diet is the gold standard. Feed a novel protein diet (e.g., duck, venison, rabbit) or hydrolyzed protein diet (proteins broken down so they don't trigger an immune response) for 8‑12 weeks. If symptoms resolve, reintroduce the original diet to confirm.

Management:

  • Novel protein diets – single protein source that your pet hasn't eaten before (e.g., venison, rabbit, kangaroo).
  • Hydrolyzed protein diets – proteins are broken down into small peptides that don't trigger allergic reactions.
  • Strict avoidance – even small amounts of the offending ingredient can cause reactions.

5. When to See a Veterinary Dermatologist

  • If symptoms don't improve with over‑the‑counter remedies.
  • If you suspect food allergies and want to pursue an elimination diet.
  • If your pet has recurrent ear infections or skin infections.
  • If your pet is severely itchy and impacting their quality of life.

6. Your Action Plan

  1. Differentiate environmental vs. food allergies (seasonality, signs, age of onset).
  2. If you suspect food allergies, start an 8‑week elimination diet with a novel or hydrolyzed protein.
  3. If environmental allergies are suspected, reduce allergen exposure and consider antihistamines or immunotherapy.
  4. Work with your vet or a veterinary dermatologist for a comprehensive treatment plan.

Final thought: Allergies are a common and treatable condition. Identifying the underlying cause – environmental or food‑related – is the key to effective management. The cost of prescription diets and immunotherapy is far less than the cost of chronic infections, repeated vet visits, and decreased quality of life for your pet.
