📊 Prevalence: Dermatitis is one of the most common reasons for veterinary visits in dogs and cats. It's estimated that up to 20‑30% of dogs and 10‑15% of cats will develop some form of dermatitis in their lifetime.

1. What Is Pet Eczema and Dermatitis?

Eczema and dermatitis are terms used to describe inflammation of the skin. They cause redness, itching, scaling, and sometimes crusty lesions. While the terms are often used interchangeably, dermatitis refers to general skin inflammation, while eczema specifically describes a chronic, itchy, scaly rash.

3. Signs of Dermatitis

  • Red, inflamed skin – often warm to the touch.
  • Intense itching – scratching, licking, or biting.
  • Dry, flaky, or scaly patches
  • Hair loss – from scratching or licking.
  • Crusty, weeping lesions – indicating secondary bacterial infection.
  • Foul odor – from yeast or bacterial overgrowth.
  • Thickened, darker skin – from chronic inflammation (hyperpigmentation).
⚠️ If your pet has open sores, intense itching, or skin odor, seek veterinary care promptly to prevent secondary infections.

4. Diagnosis

  • Physical examination – your vet will examine the skin and look for patterns.
  • Skin scraping – to rule out parasites (mites, fleas).
  • Cytology – swabs to identify bacteria or yeast.
  • Allergy testing – intradermal or serum tests for environmental or food allergies.
  • Elimination diet – for food allergies, an 8‑12 week trial.

5. Treatment Options

  • Topical treatments – medicated shampoos, creams, and sprays (anti‑inflammatory, antibacterial, antifungal).
  • Oral medications – antihistamines, steroids, immunosuppressants (cyclosporine, oclacitinib).
  • Antibiotics or antifungals – for secondary infections.
  • Dietary changes – hypoallergenic or prescription diets for food allergies.
  • Immunotherapy – allergy shots for atopic dermatitis.

7. What to Avoid

  • Over‑the‑counter human creams – many contain steroids or other ingredients that are harmful to pets.
  • Ignoring secondary infections – untreated infections can spread and become severe.
  • Using harsh soaps – can strip natural oils and worsen dermatitis.
  • Not addressing the underlying cause – treating symptoms without identifying the root cause leads to recurrence.

8. Your Action Plan

  1. If you notice itching, redness, or hair loss, schedule a vet exam.
  2. Work with your vet to identify the type of dermatitis (allergy, infection, etc.).
  3. Follow the treatment protocol (medications, medicated shampoos, dietary changes).
  4. Prevent recurrence by managing underlying allergies (diet, environment, flea control).

Final thought: Dermatitis is common but treatable. Early diagnosis and proper management prevent discomfort, secondary infections, and long‑term skin damage. Work with your vet to identify the cause and treat it effectively – your pet's skin will thank you.
