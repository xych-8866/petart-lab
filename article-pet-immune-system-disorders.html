When 5‑year‑old Golden Retriever, Bella, developed crusty, ulcerated skin lesions on her nose, face, and ears, her owner thought it was a stubborn skin infection. After multiple courses of antibiotics failed, a skin biopsy revealed pemphigus foliaceus – an autoimmune disease where the immune system attacks the skin cells. "Now I understand why she needed such aggressive treatment."

📊 Prevalence: Autoimmune diseases are relatively rare but serious conditions in dogs and cats. They can affect the skin, blood, joints, or multiple organ systems. Early diagnosis is critical for successful management.

1. What Are Autoimmune Diseases?

In autoimmune diseases, the immune system fails to distinguish between healthy cells and foreign invaders, attacking the body's own tissues. This can affect almost any organ or system, including the skin, blood cells, joints, kidneys, and nervous system.

3. Signs of Immunodeficiency (Weakened Immune System)

  • Recurrent or chronic infections – ear, skin, respiratory, or urinary infections that don't resolve.
  • Poor response to antibiotics – infections that keep coming back.
  • Slow wound healing – cuts, scrapes, or surgical incisions that take longer than normal to heal.
  • Failure to thrive – reduced growth, poor coat, and low energy.
  • Susceptibility to opportunistic infections – infections that healthy pets would normally fight off.

4. Causes of Immunodeficiency

  • Primary (genetic) immunodeficiencies – inherited conditions affecting immune function.
  • Secondary immunodeficiencies – caused by diseases, medications, or environmental factors.
  • Infectious diseases – viruses like FeLV (cats), FIV (cats), distemper (dogs).
  • Corticosteroids or immunosuppressive drugs – long‑term use can suppress immune function.
  • Malnutrition – deficiencies in essential nutrients and vitamins.

5. Diagnosis

  • Complete blood count (CBC) – to check for anemia, low platelets, or abnormal white blood cells.
  • Serum biochemistry – to assess organ function (kidney, liver).
  • Skin biopsy – for skin‑attacking autoimmune diseases.
  • Coagulation tests – for immune‑mediated thrombocytopenia (ITP).
  • Specialist referral – veterinary internal medicine or dermatology specialist.

6. Treatment Options

  • Immunosuppressive drugs – corticosteroids (prednisolone), cyclosporine, or other immunomodulators.
  • Supportive care – IV fluids, blood transfusions (for IMHA or ITP), and hospitalization.
  • Topical therapy – medicated shampoos, ointments, and creams for skin lesions.
  • Dietary support – high‑quality, easily digestible diets to support recovery and immune function.
  • Management of underlying causes – treating infections or addressing environmental factors.

8. What to Avoid

  • Ignoring early signs – autoimmune diseases can progress rapidly.
  • Stopping medications abruptly – immunosuppressive drugs must be tapered slowly under veterinary supervision.
  • Self‑treating with over‑the‑counter remedies – can interfere with prescribed treatments.
  • Delaying specialist referral – early involvement of a veterinary specialist improves outcomes.

9. Your Action Plan

  1. If you notice skin lesions, bruising, bleeding, or unexplained weakness, see your vet promptly.
  2. Follow your vet's diagnostic recommendations (blood tests, skin biopsy).
  3. If autoimmune disease is diagnosed, work closely with your vet or a specialist to manage treatment.
  4. Provide supportive care – nutritious diet, stress reduction, and regular monitoring.

Final thought: Autoimmune diseases are complex and often require lifelong management. The cost of immunosuppressive medications and specialist care can be significant ($100‑$500/month), but they are essential for controlling the disease and maintaining quality of life. Early diagnosis and treatment offer the best chance for remission and long‑term survival.
