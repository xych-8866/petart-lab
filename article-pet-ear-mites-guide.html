📊 Prevalence: Ear mites (Otodectes cynotis) are one of the most common external parasites in cats and dogs, especially in kittens and puppies. They are highly contagious between animals.

1. What Are Ear Mites?

Ear mites are tiny, spider‑like parasites that live in the ear canal. They feed on ear wax, skin debris, and blood, causing intense itching and inflammation. They are highly contagious and spread through direct contact with an infected animal or contaminated bedding.

2. Signs of Ear Mites

  • Dark, crumbly debris – often described as looking like coffee grounds.
  • Head shaking or tilting – trying to dislodge the irritation.
  • Scratching at ears – sometimes causing self‑inflicted wounds.
  • Redness and swelling – in the ear canal and outer ear.
  • Foul odor – from secondary bacterial or yeast infections.
  • Skin irritation – around the ears, neck, or tail (in severe infestations).
⚠️ If left untreated, ear mites can cause chronic ear infections, eardrum damage, and hearing loss. See your vet promptly.

3. How Pets Get Ear Mites

  • Direct contact – with an infested animal (most common).
  • Contaminated bedding – mites can survive for a short time in the environment.
  • From the mother – kittens and puppies can get mites from their mothers.
  • Outdoor exposure – stray animals or feral colonies.

4. Diagnosis

  • Physical examination – your vet will examine the ear canal with an otoscope.
  • Ear cytology – a swab of ear debris is examined under a microscope to identify mites.
  • Rule out other causes – bacterial or yeast infections can mimic ear mite symptoms.

6. Treating the Environment and Multi‑Pet Households

  • Treat all pets – ear mites are highly contagious; all pets in the household should be treated, even if asymptomatic.
  • Wash bedding – in hot water to kill mites in the environment.
  • Vacuum – thoroughly clean carpets and furniture.
  • Isolate infested pets – temporarily separate from healthy pets during treatment.

8. What to Avoid

  • Using human ear drops – can be toxic to pets.
  • Ignoring the problem – mites don't go away on their own.
  • Only treating one pet – all animals in the household need treatment.

9. Your Action Plan

  1. If you notice dark debris, scratching, or head shaking, see your vet for diagnosis.
  2. Follow the treatment protocol (drops, topical, or oral).
  3. Treat all pets in the household and clean the environment.
  4. Use monthly prevention (Revolution, Advantage Multi) to prevent recurrence.

Final thought: Ear mites are common but highly treatable. Early diagnosis and treatment prevent complications and discomfort. If you have multiple pets, treat them all – and always clean the environment to prevent reinfestation.
