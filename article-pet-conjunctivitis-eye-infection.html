📊 Prevalence: Conjunctivitis is one of the most common eye conditions in dogs and cats. In cats, up to 80% of upper respiratory infections include conjunctivitis as a symptom.

1. Signs of Conjunctivitis

  • Red or pink eye – inflammation of the conjunctiva.
  • Discharge – clear, yellow, or green (mucoid or purulent).
  • Squinting or blinking excessively – indicates pain or irritation.
  • Pawing at the eye – rubbing the eye with a paw or against furniture.
  • Swelling around the eye – in severe cases.
  • Third eyelid protrusion – the inner eyelid may become visible.
⚠️ If your pet has a painful eye, squinting, or a green/yellow discharge, see a vet promptly – it may indicate a corneal ulcer or serious infection.

2. Common Causes

  • Allergies – seasonal or food allergies can trigger conjunctivitis.
  • Bacterial infections – secondary to other conditions.
  • Viral infections – especially in cats (feline herpesvirus).
  • Foreign bodies – dust, grass seeds, or debris.
  • Dry eye (keratoconjunctivitis sicca) – lack of tear production, more common in dogs.
  • Trauma – scratches or injuries to the eye.

3. How Vets Diagnose

  • Physical examination – visual inspection of the eye and surrounding structures.
  • Fluorescein stain test – checks for corneal ulcers or scratches.
  • Schirmer tear test – measures tear production (for dry eye).
  • Cytology or culture – if bacterial infection is suspected.

4. Treatment Options

  • Antibiotic eye drops or ointment – for bacterial infections. Prescription required.
  • Anti‑inflammatory eye drops – to reduce swelling and pain.
  • Artificial tears – for mild irritation or dry eye.
  • Antiviral medications – for feline herpesvirus.
  • Warm compresses – to gently clean crusty discharge.
  • Oral antibiotics or anti‑inflammatories – for systemic infections.

5. How to Clean Your Pet's Eyes at Home

  1. Use a soft, damp cotton ball or gauze pad.
  2. Gently wipe from the inner corner outward (toward the ear).
  3. Use a fresh cotton ball for each eye to prevent cross‑contamination.
  4. Never wipe directly on the eyeball or use cotton swabs.
  5. If using a pet eye wash, follow product instructions.

6. When to See the Vet

  • Immediately if: The eye is painful (squinting, pawing), there is green/yellow discharge, or the eye is swollen.
  • Within 24‑48 hours if: Redness persists for more than 2 days, or your pet has a history of eye problems.
  • Do not use human eye drops – some contain steroids or other ingredients that are harmful to pets.

8. Your Action Plan

  1. If you notice redness or discharge, clean the eye gently and monitor.
  2. If symptoms worsen or are accompanied by pain, see your vet promptly.
  3. For chronic issues, ask your vet about allergy testing or long‑term eye care.
