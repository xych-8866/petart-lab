📊 Prevalence: Ear infections (otitis externa) are one of the most common reasons for veterinary visits in dogs, affecting up to 20% of dogs. Certain breeds (Cocker Spaniels, Golden Retrievers, Poodles) are more predisposed.

1. Early Signs (Don't Ignore)

  • Head shaking or tilting – one of the earliest signs.
  • Scratching at ears or rubbing face on the floor
  • Redness or swelling of the ear canal
  • Dark brown, yellow, or bloody discharge
  • Foul odor from the ear
  • Pain when touching the ear or around the head
⚠️ If your pet shows signs of a head tilt, circling, or loss of balance, seek immediate veterinary care – this may indicate a middle ear infection or neurological issue.

2. Causes of Ear Infections

  • Bacteria (Staphylococcus, Pseudomonas, Proteus) – often secondary to allergies or moisture.
  • Yeast (Malassezia pachydermatis) – thrives in warm, moist environments.
  • Allergies (food or environmental) – underlying cause in 60‑80% of recurrent cases.
  • Anatomy (floppy ears, narrow ear canals) – restricts airflow, traps moisture.
  • Excessive moisture – from swimming, bathing, or humidity.
  • Foreign bodies (grass seeds, debris) – irritate the ear canal.

3. How Vets Diagnose Ear Infections

  • Otoscopy – visual examination of the ear canal using an otoscope.
  • Cytology (ear swab) – identifies bacteria, yeast, and inflammatory cells.
  • Culture and sensitivity – for chronic or resistant infections.
  • Imaging (CT or X‑ray) – for middle or inner ear involvement.

4. Treatment Options

  • Ear cleaning solutions – to remove debris and discharge, used daily during treatment.
  • Topical medications (ear drops) – containing antibiotics, antifungals, or steroids.
  • Oral medications (antibiotics, antifungals, anti‑inflammatories) – for severe or middle ear infections.
  • Injectable medications – for chronic or resistant infections.
  • Dietary management – if food allergies are the underlying cause, switching to a hydrolyzed or novel protein diet.

6. Prevention: Weekly Ear Cleaning Routine

  1. Fill the ear canal with a veterinary ear cleaner.
  2. Massage the base of the ear gently for 30 seconds (you'll hear a squishing sound).
  3. Allow your pet to shake out the excess.
  4. Wipe the outer ear with a cotton ball (never use cotton swabs inside the ear).
  5. Repeat weekly, or after swimming/bathing.

7. What to Avoid

  • Using cotton swabs inside the ear – pushes debris deeper and can damage the eardrum.
  • Using vinegar or alcohol – can irritate inflamed tissue.
  • Ignoring underlying allergies – recurrent infections require investigation and dietary changes.
  • Stopping treatment too early – finish the full course of medication.

8. Your Action Plan

  1. If you notice signs of an ear infection, see your vet for diagnosis.
  2. Follow the prescribed treatment (cleaning + medication) consistently.
  3. After recovery, start a weekly cleaning routine to prevent recurrence.
  4. If infections recur, discuss allergy testing and dietary changes with your vet.
