Pet CPR & Emergency First Aid: Step-by-Step Guide (2026)

When 7-year-old Beagle, Cooper, collapsed in the backyard after choking on a toy, his owner panicked. She didn't know CPR. By the time she got to the vet, it was too late. "I wish I had learned pet CPR," she says. "It could have saved his life."

📊 Fact: Pet CPR can double or triple the chance of survival in cardiac arrest. Every pet owner should learn basic CPR and first aid – it takes only 15 minutes to learn and could save your pet's life.
🚨 BEFORE YOU START CPR
1. Make sure the environment is safe for you and your pet.
2. Check for breathing and a heartbeat.
3. If your pet is breathing or has a pulse, DO NOT perform CPR – it can cause harm.
4. Call your vet or emergency clinic immediately.
5. Only start CPR if your pet is unresponsive, not breathing, and has no heartbeat.

1. When to Perform CPR

CPR is only for pets that are unconscious, not breathing, and have no heartbeat. If your pet is breathing or has a pulse, do not perform CPR – it can cause serious injury.

Signs to check:

  • Unconsciousness: Not responding to your voice or touch.
  • Breathing: Look for chest movement. Listen for breath sounds. Feel for breath on your hand.
  • Heartbeat: Place your hand on the chest behind the elbow, or check the femoral artery (inner thigh).

2. What You'll Need

  • A flat, stable surface
  • A towel or blanket (optional, for padding)
  • A phone to call for emergency help
  • Knowledge of the CPR ratios (see below)

3. Step-by-Step CPR for Dogs and Cats

Step 1: Position the pet

Lay your pet on their right side on a flat, stable surface. Ensure the airway is clear – open the mouth and check for any obstructions (food, toys, etc.).

Step 2: Rescue breaths

Close the pet's mouth and breathe into the nose (not the mouth). Watch for the chest to rise. Give 2 rescue breaths (each breath should take about 1 second).

For cats and small dogs: Cover the mouth and nose with your mouth and breathe gently.

For medium to large dogs: Cover the nose with your mouth and breathe firmly.

Step 3: Chest compressions

Place one hand over the ribcage, just behind the elbow. For large dogs, you may need to use two hands.

Compression depth: About 1/3 to 1/2 the width of the chest.

Compression rate: 100-120 compressions per minute (think of the song "Stayin' Alive" by the Bee Gees – it's the perfect tempo).

Step 4: Compression-to-ventilation ratio

Perform cycles of 30 compressions followed by 2 rescue breaths.

For cats and small dogs: You may need to compress with two fingers instead of a whole hand.

5. When to Stop CPR

  • Your pet starts breathing on their own
  • Your pet regains a heartbeat
  • You are too exhausted to continue
  • Veterinary help arrives
  • After 20 minutes of CPR without response (prognosis is poor)

6. What to Do After CPR

If your pet regains consciousness, keep them warm and calm. Transport them to the vet immediately – even if they seem fine, internal injuries or underlying causes need to be addressed.

7. Real Owner Success Story

"My 8-year-old Pug, Gus, collapsed during a walk on a hot day. I had learned pet CPR in a class and was able to perform chest compressions and rescue breathing until we got to the vet. Gus survived and made a full recovery. Learning CPR saved his life." – Sarah, Florida

8. What to Avoid

  • Performing CPR on a pet that is breathing or has a pulse – can cause serious injury
  • Bending the neck backward – can cause airway obstruction
  • Performing CPR on a pet with severe chest trauma – can worsen injuries
  • Giving up too quickly – continue until help arrives or you are too exhausted to continue

9. Your Action Plan

  1. Learn pet CPR and first aid – take a class or watch a certified video.
  2. Keep emergency contact numbers (vet, emergency clinic) easily accessible.
  3. Check the environment before starting CPR – ensure it's safe.
  4. Practice the technique on a mannequin if available – it builds confidence.

Final thought: Pet CPR is a skill every pet owner should know. It takes only 15 minutes to learn and could save your pet's life. Take a class, watch a video, or read a guide – and practice the technique. You never know when you might need it.
