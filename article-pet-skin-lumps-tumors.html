Pet Skin Lumps & Tumors: When to Worry and What to Do (2026)

  • Red flags: rapid growth, ulceration, redness, non‑healing wounds

When 8‑year‑old Boxer, Max, developed a small pink bump on his leg, his owner thought it was just a wart. Over two months, the bump grew, became red and ulcerated. The vet performed a fine needle aspirate and diagnosed a mast cell tumor – a common but potentially aggressive skin cancer in dogs. Because it was caught early, Max underwent surgery with clean margins and is now cancer‑free. "I almost ignored it," his owner says. "Now I check him weekly."

📊 Prevalence: Skin tumors are the most common tumors in dogs and the second most common in cats. Up to 50% of older dogs will develop at least one skin lump.

2. Red Flags (When to See a Vet Immediately)

  • Rapid growth – doubling in size in weeks.
  • Ulceration or bleeding – open sore that doesn't heal.
  • Redness, swelling, or pain – signs of inflammation or infection.
  • Firm, fixed lump – attached to underlying tissue.
  • Multiple lumps appearing suddenly – may indicate systemic disease.
  • Lump in a high‑risk location – mouth, nail beds, anus, or between toes.
⚠️ Do not wait – early diagnosis saves lives. A simple needle aspirate (FNA) can often give an answer within 30 minutes.

3. How Vets Diagnose Skin Lumps

  • Fine Needle Aspirate (FNA) – a needle is inserted into the lump to collect cells for cytology. Quick, inexpensive, and usually sufficient.
  • Biopsy – removal of a small piece of tissue for histopathology. Gold standard for diagnosing tumor type and grade.
  • Imaging – X‑rays, ultrasound, CT scan to check for metastasis.
  • Staging – lymph node aspiration, bloodwork to assess overall health.

4. Treatment Options

  • Surgical excision – the most common treatment for localized tumors. Requires clean margins (removing a border of normal tissue).
  • Radiation therapy – for tumors that are difficult to remove surgically (e.g., on the face, paw).
  • Chemotherapy – for metastatic or high‑grade tumors.
  • Watch‑and‑wait – for small, benign‑appearing lumps that are not bothering the pet.
  • Targeted therapy (e.g., Palladia for mast cell tumors) – oral medication for certain types.

6. How to Perform Monthly Skin Checks (At Home)

  1. Run your hands over your pet's entire body, feeling for any lumps, bumps, or scabs.
  2. Check between toes, under armpits, inside ears, and around the mouth and anus.
  3. Note any changes in size, color, or texture of existing lumps.
  4. Take photos with a ruler for scale – helps track growth.
  5. Schedule vet visits for any new or changing lumps.

7. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Assuming a lump is "just a fatty tumor" – lipomas are common, but without an FNA you can't be sure.
  • Waiting too long – most tumors are easier to treat when small.
  • Using home remedies (e.g., essential oils, wart removers) on skin lumps – can cause irritation, infection, and delay diagnosis.
  • Ignoring non‑healing wounds – may be a sign of skin cancer.

8. Your Action Plan

  1. Perform a monthly skin check on your pet.
  2. If you find a new lump or an existing lump changes, schedule a vet appointment for an FNA.
  3. Do not delay – early diagnosis leads to better outcomes.

Final thought: Most skin lumps in dogs and cats are benign, but the only way to know is through cytology or biopsy. An FNA is quick, inexpensive, and can save your pet from a treatable cancer. Don't ignore lumps – check them, document them, and act early.
