📊 Prevalence: Cataracts are the leading cause of blindness in dogs and cats. Diabetic dogs have a 70-80% chance of developing cataracts within 6‑12 months of diagnosis. Breeds at higher risk: Miniature Poodles, Cocker Spaniels, Boston Terriers, Siberian Huskies, and certain cat breeds (Persians, Himalayans).

1. What Are Cataracts?

A cataract is a clouding of the eye's lens, which is normally clear. This cloudiness prevents light from reaching the retina, leading to progressive vision loss. Cataracts can be:

  • Diabetic – rapid onset (weeks to months).
  • Congenital – present at birth or develops in young dogs.
  • Age‑related (senile) – slow progression over years.
  • Trauma or infection‑related – less common.

It's important to distinguish cataracts from **nuclear sclerosis**, a normal aging change where the lens becomes slightly greyish but does not significantly affect vision. A veterinary ophthalmologist can differentiate the two.

2. Early Signs

  • Cloudy or white appearance in the pupil – often the first sign.
  • Bumping into objects – especially at night or in low light.
  • Hesitation on stairs or in unfamiliar environments.
  • Squinting or pawing at eyes – may indicate pain or inflammation (uveitis).
⚠️ Cataracts can lead to secondary glaucoma or lens luxation (dislocation), both emergencies. Seek specialist care early.

3. Diagnosis

A veterinary ophthalmologist will perform a complete eye exam, including:

  • Slit‑lamp biomicroscopy – to see the lens and cataract type.
  • Intraocular pressure measurement – to rule out glaucoma.
  • Electroretinography (ERG) – to assess retinal function (important before surgery).
  • Ultrasound – if the lens is so cloudy that the retina cannot be visualized.

4. Treatment Options

Non‑Surgical Management

For early cataracts or when surgery isn't an option (e.g., advanced age, other health issues), management focuses on:

  • Antioxidant eye drops (aldose reductase inhibitors) – may slow progression in diabetic dogs (N‑acetylcarnosine, marketed as Ocuvet).
  • Anti‑inflammatory drops – to control uveitis (inflammation inside the eye).
  • Adapting the environment – use non‑slip mats, avoid rearranging furniture, use nightlights.

Surgical Treatment: Cataract Surgery (Phacoemulsification)

This is the only curative treatment. The cloudy lens is broken up and removed (ultrasound), and an artificial lens (intraocular lens, IOL) is implanted. Success rate is high (90%+), provided the retina is healthy.

  • Cost: $2,500‑$5,000 per eye (may include pre‑op ERG, ultrasound, post‑op medications).
  • Post‑op care: Frequent eye drops (multiple types), e‑collar, restricted activity, and rechecks.
  • Outcome: Restores functional vision, but may not be 20/20.

5. Preventing Cataracts

  • Control diabetes – tight glucose control significantly reduces cataract risk.
  • Regular eye exams – annual check‑ups, especially for high‑risk breeds.
  • Antioxidant supplementation – omega‑3s, vitamin E, vitamin C, lutein, and zeaxanthin may support eye health.
  • Protect eyes from trauma – avoid rough play, keep away from sharp objects.

7. What to Avoid

  • Over‑the‑counter "cure" drops – only veterinary‑approved products.
  • Ignoring signs of inflammation – uveitis can cause glaucoma and blindness.
  • Delaying surgery – early surgery has better outcomes.

8. Your Action Plan

  1. If you notice cloudy eyes, schedule an appointment with a veterinary ophthalmologist.
  2. Discuss surgery options if your pet is a good candidate.
  3. Manage underlying conditions (especially diabetes) to slow progression.

Final thought: Cataracts are not a death sentence for vision. With modern surgery, most pets regain functional vision. Early diagnosis and specialist consultation are critical. If surgery isn't an option, antioxidants and environmental modifications can help maintain a good quality of life.
