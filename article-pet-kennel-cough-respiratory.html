📊 Prevalence: Kennel cough (infectious tracheobronchitis) is one of the most common respiratory infections in dogs. It spreads rapidly in environments where dogs are in close contact – boarding kennels, dog parks, training classes, and shelters.

1. What Is Kennel Cough?

Kennel cough is a highly contagious respiratory infection caused by a combination of viruses and bacteria. The most common culprits are Bordetella bronchiseptica (bacteria) and canine parainfluenza virus. It affects the trachea (windpipe) and bronchi, causing inflammation and a characteristic dry, hacking cough.

2. Signs and Symptoms

  • Dry, hacking cough – often described as a "goose honk" or like something is stuck in the throat.
  • Gagging or retching – may bring up white foam.
  • Runny nose and sneezing – especially in early stages.
  • Lethargy and reduced appetite – mild in most cases.
  • Fever – uncommon but possible in severe cases.
⚠️ If your dog has a cough, keep them away from other dogs. Kennel cough is highly contagious and can spread through the air or via contaminated surfaces.

3. How Dogs Catch It

  • Airborne droplets – coughing and sneezing release infected particles.
  • Direct contact – nose-to-nose contact with an infected dog.
  • Contaminated surfaces – shared toys, food bowls, and water bowls.
  • Stress factors – stress from boarding, travel, or overcrowding weakens the immune system, making dogs more susceptible.

4. When to See the Vet

Most cases of kennel cough are mild and resolve on their own within 7‑10 days. However, you should see a vet if:

  • The cough is severe or persistent (> 10 days).
  • Your dog has a fever, lethargy, or loss of appetite.
  • There is green or bloody nasal discharge.
  • Your dog is having difficulty breathing.
  • Your dog is very young, very old, or has pre‑existing health conditions.

6. Prevention: Vaccination

The Bordetella vaccine is recommended for dogs that are frequently boarded, attend daycare, or visit dog parks. It is not always 100% effective, but it significantly reduces the severity of symptoms.

  • Intranasal vaccine – administered as drops into the nose. Provides rapid protection (within 48‑72 hours).
  • Injectable vaccine – given under the skin. Takes 2‑3 weeks to become effective.
  • Oral vaccine – newer option, given by mouth.
  • Booster frequency – typically every 6‑12 months, depending on risk.

8. What to Avoid

  • Exposing your dog to other dogs while coughing – highly contagious.
  • Using human cough medicines – some contain xylitol or other toxic ingredients.
  • Ignoring a persistent cough – can progress to pneumonia.

9. Your Action Plan

  1. If your dog has a cough, isolate them from other dogs immediately.
  2. Monitor for other signs (fever, lethargy, appetite changes).
  3. If symptoms are severe or persist > 7‑10 days, see your vet.
  4. Consider Bordetella vaccination before boarding, daycare, or dog park visits.

Final thought: Kennel cough is common, highly contagious, and usually mild – but it can become serious in vulnerable dogs. Prevention is key: vaccinate before high‑risk activities, and if your dog develops a cough, isolate them to prevent spreading it to others.
