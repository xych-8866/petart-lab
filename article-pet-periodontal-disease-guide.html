📊 Prevalence: Periodontal disease is the most common health condition in dogs and cats. By age 3, over 80% of dogs and 70% of cats have some form of periodontal disease. It's not just a "dental" issue – bacteria from the mouth enters the bloodstream and can damage the heart, kidneys, and liver.

1. What Is Periodontal Disease?

Periodontal disease is inflammation of the tissues that support the teeth – including the gums, periodontal ligament, and alveolar bone. It starts with plaque (a soft film of bacteria) that mineralizes into tartar (calculus) within 3‑5 days. Tartar provides a rough surface for more plaque to attach, leading to gum inflammation (gingivitis) and eventually bone loss (periodontitis).

3. Signs of Periodontal Disease

  • Bad breath (halitosis) – often the first sign.
  • Yellow or brown tartar on teeth
  • Red, swollen, or bleeding gums
  • Pawing at the mouth or rubbing face on the floor
  • Dropping food, eating slowly, or refusing food
  • Loose teeth or missing teeth
⚠️ If you notice any of these signs, schedule a veterinary dental exam. Untreated periodontal disease can lead to systemic health problems.

4. Diagnosis and Treatment

  • Physical exam – your vet will examine the teeth and gums, checking for mobility, bleeding, and tartar.
  • Dental X‑rays – necessary to assess bone loss and tooth roots (often 50‑70% of disease is below the gumline).
  • Professional dental cleaning (under anesthesia) – scaling above and below the gumline, polishing, and extracting loose teeth.
  • Anesthesia is necessary – for patient safety, accurate X‑rays, and thorough cleaning. Modern anesthesia is very safe with pre‑anesthetic bloodwork.

5. Home Care: Prevention is Key

  • Daily brushing – the gold standard for preventing plaque and tartar. Use a soft‑bristled brush and pet‑specific toothpaste (never human).
  • VOHC‑approved dental chews – help reduce plaque and tartar (supplement, not a replacement for brushing).
  • Water additives – some help reduce plaque formation.
  • Dental diets – specially formulated to help clean teeth during chewing.

7. What NOT to Do

  • Don't ignore bad breath – it's a sign of dental disease.
  • Don't use human toothpaste – contains xylitol (toxic) and fluoride (upset stomach).
  • Don't attempt to scrape tartar at home – you can damage the enamel and gums.
  • Don't skip annual dental exams – your vet can detect early signs before they become severe.

8. Your Action Plan

  1. Start daily brushing – even 2‑3 times per week is better than nothing.
  2. Use a VOHC‑approved dental chew or water additive.
  3. Schedule a veterinary dental exam at least once a year (more often for high‑risk breeds).
  4. If your pet has signs of dental disease, book a professional cleaning.

Final thought: Periodontal disease is preventable and manageable. A professional cleaning costs $500‑$1,500, but the cost of extractions and treating systemic disease is far higher. Daily brushing and annual dental exams are the best investments you can make in your pet's health.
