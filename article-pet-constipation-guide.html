📊 Prevalence: Constipation is a common gastrointestinal issue in dogs and cats, especially in senior pets, long‑haired cats (hairballs), and pets with chronic health conditions. Left untreated, it can progress to obstipation – a severe, unresponsive form of constipation.

1. Signs of Constipation

  • Straining to defecate – with little or no stool produced.
  • Hard, dry stools – small, pebble‑like pieces.
  • Decreased appetite – discomfort often reduces eating.
  • Lethargy – reduced energy and activity.
  • Abdominal pain – whining, hunched posture, or sensitivity to touch.
  • Vomiting – in severe cases, from intestinal backup.
⚠️ If your pet has not produced stool in more than 48‑72 hours, or if they are straining painfully, see your vet immediately. Obstipation (complete blockage) is a medical emergency.

2. Common Causes of Constipation

CauseDescription
DehydrationInsufficient water intake leads to hard, dry stool
Dietary factorsLow‑fiber diet, excessive bone meal, or sudden diet changes
Hairballs (cats)Ingested hair can accumulate and block the intestine
MedicationsSome pain medications, antihistamines, and diuretics can cause constipation
Underlying diseaseKidney disease, diabetes, hypothyroidism, or megacolon
Stress or anxietyChanges in routine, environment, or travel can affect bowel movements

3. Home Remedies for Mild Constipation

  • Increase water intake – use water fountains, wet food, and multiple water bowls.
  • Pumpkin puree – 1‑2 tablespoons of plain, unsweetened pumpkin puree adds soluble fiber and moisture.
  • Fiber supplements – psyllium husk (e.g., Metamucil) or canned pumpkin can help regulate stool.
  • Gentle exercise – light walking can stimulate bowel movement.
  • Hairball remedies (cats) – petroleum‑based gels help hair pass through the digestive tract.

4. When to See the Vet

  • No stool for more than 48‑72 hours.
  • Severe straining with no production (obstipation).
  • Vomiting, lethargy, or loss of appetite.
  • Abdominal swelling or pain.
  • Suspected foreign body ingestion.

Veterinary treatments may include: enemas, hydration therapy (subcutaneous or IV), manual removal of impacted feces, and medications (laxatives, prokinetics).

5. Prevention Strategies

  • Maintain adequate hydration – water fountains and wet food encourage drinking.
  • Feed a high‑fiber diet – choose pet foods with moderate fiber content, and add pumpkin or psyllium if needed.
  • Regular grooming (cats) – reduces hair ingestion and hairball formation.
  • Routine exercise – encourages normal bowel motility.
  • Manage stress – provide routine, environmental enrichment, and positive reinforcement.

6. Real Owner Success Story

7. What to Avoid

  • Human laxatives – many are toxic to pets.
  • Mineral oil – can be aspirated into the lungs and cause pneumonia.
  • Ignoring the problem – constipation can lead to obstipation, megacolon, or intestinal rupture.

8. Your Action Plan

  1. Monitor your pet's bowel habits – know what's normal for them.
  2. If you notice straining, start with hydration and pumpkin puree.
  3. If no improvement in 24 hours, or if your pet is distressed, see your vet.
  4. For chronic constipation, work with your vet to create a long‑term prevention plan.

Final thought: Constipation is uncomfortable and can become dangerous if ignored. Early intervention with hydration, fiber, and veterinary care can prevent serious complications. A little pumpkin puree and a water fountain can go a long way in keeping your pet regular.
