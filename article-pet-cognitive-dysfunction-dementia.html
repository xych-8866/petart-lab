When 12‑year‑old cat, Oliver, started pacing at night, howling for no apparent reason, and forgetting where his food bowl was, his owner thought it was just old age. But the vet diagnosed cognitive dysfunction syndrome (CDS) – a condition similar to Alzheimer's disease in humans. 📊 Prevalence: CDS affects up to 50% of dogs and cats over 10 years of age, and nearly 70% of pets over 15 years. Recognizing early signs can improve quality of life and slow progression.

⚠️ If your senior pet shows multiple signs, schedule a veterinary exam to rule out other causes (arthritis, vision loss, UTI).

2. How Vets Diagnose CDS

  • Physical exam and bloodwork – to rule out other medical conditions (thyroid, kidney, liver, diabetes).
  • Neurological exam – to assess cognitive function and rule out brain tumors.
  • Behavioral questionnaire – often used to assess the severity and track progression.
  • Imaging (MRI/CT) – if a brain tumor is suspected (less common).

5. When to Consider Medication

  • Selegiline (Anipryl) – the only FDA‑approved medication for CDS in dogs. Works by increasing dopamine levels and may improve cognitive function.
  • Gabapentin or trazodone – may be used to manage anxiety and nighttime restlessness.
  • Always discuss medication options with your vet – they are not appropriate for every pet.

6. What to Avoid

  • Ignoring early signs – early intervention slows progression.
  • Punishing your pet – house soiling is not a behavioral issue; it's a medical condition.
  • Making sudden changes – routines help reduce anxiety and confusion.

7. Your Action Plan

  1. If you notice signs of CDS, schedule a veterinary exam to rule out other causes.
  2. Start a brain‑support diet and supplements (SAMe, omega‑3s).
  3. Implement environmental enrichment (puzzle toys, consistent routines, nightlights).
  4. If symptoms persist, discuss medication with your vet.
