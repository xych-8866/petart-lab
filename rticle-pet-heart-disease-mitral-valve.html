📊 Prevalence: DMVD affects up to 85% of older small breed dogs (Cavalier King Charles Spaniels, Dachshunds, Miniature Poodles, Chihuahuas). It's the leading cause of heart failure in dogs.

1. Early Signs (Often Subtle)

  • Heart murmur – often detected during routine physical exam before symptoms appear.
  • Reduced exercise tolerance – tiring more easily on walks.
  • Coughing at night – often mistaken for kennel cough or allergies.
  • Increased respiratory rate – resting rate > 30 breaths/minute.
  • Fainting or collapse – exercise‑induced syncope.
  • Abdominal swelling – fluid accumulation (ascites) in advanced stages.
⚠️ If your small breed dog has a heart murmur, annual echocardiograms are essential to monitor progression.

2. The ACVIM Staging System

AHigh‑risk breed, no heart murmurAnnual check‑up

CSigns of heart failure (cough, breathing difficulty)Add diuretics (furosemide) and ACE inhibitors
DAdvanced, refractory heart failureSpecialist care, aggressive management

3. Medications That Can Extend Life

  • Pimobendan (Vetmedin) – the only drug proven to slow disease progression and extend survival in stage B2 dogs. Also used in stage C and D for symptom control.
  • ACE inhibitors (enalapril, benazepril) – reduce blood pressure and decrease heart workload.
  • Diuretics (furosemide) – remove fluid from lungs and abdomen (used in heart failure).
  • Spironolactone – potassium‑sparing diuretic, often added in advanced cases.

4. Monitoring at Home (Respiratory Rate)

The best way to detect early heart failure is to track your dog's resting respiratory rate (RRR). Count breaths for 15 seconds while your dog is sleeping (not panting). Multiply by 4.

  • Normal: 15‑30 breaths/min
  • Mildly elevated: 30‑40 – consult your vet
  • Significant elevation: > 40 – potential heart failure, seek vet advice immediately

Keep a log to share with your vet.

6. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Ignoring a heart murmur – annual echocardiograms are essential.
  • Delaying medication – starting pimobendan at stage B2 slows disease progression.
  • Overlooking exercise restriction – high‑intensity exercise can exacerbate heart strain.
  • Giving human heart medications – some are toxic to dogs; always use veterinary‑approved drugs.

7. Your Action Plan

  1. If your small breed dog has a heart murmur, schedule an echocardiogram to determine stage.
  2. If stage B2, discuss starting pimobendan with your vet.
  3. Monitor resting respiratory rate weekly – track changes.
  4. Schedule annual rechecks, or more frequently if symptoms worsen.

Final thought: DMVD is a chronic, progressive disease, but with early detection and appropriate medication, dogs can live years of quality life. Don't wait for symptoms – annual heart screening for high‑risk breeds is the best investment you can make.
