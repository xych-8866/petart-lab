1. Early Warning Signs (Don't Ignore)

  • Increased thirst and urination (polydipsia/polyuria) – the most common early sign.
  • Weight loss despite normal or increased appetite – the body can't use glucose, so it breaks down fat and muscle.
  • Cloudy eyes (cataracts) – common in diabetic dogs.
  • Recurring infections (urinary tract, skin) – high blood sugar weakens immunity.
  • Lethargy, vomiting, dehydration – later stage signs.
⚠️ If you see these signs, see your vet immediately. Early diagnosis dramatically improves outcomes.

2. Diagnosis & Initial Treatment

Your vet will run bloodwork (high blood glucose, elevated fructosamine) and a urinalysis (glucose in urine). An in‑hospital glucose curve (glucose readings every 2 hours over 12‑24 hours) determines the starting insulin dose.

  • Insulin types: Vetsulin (dogs), ProZinc (cats), Lantus (cats).
  • Initial hospitalization: Some pets need a few days to stabilize.

3. At‑Home Insulin Injections (Step‑by‑Step)

  1. Feed a meal (insulin is given with or right after meals).
  2. Gather supplies: insulin vial, syringe, alcohol wipe, sharps container.
  3. Roll (don't shake) the vial to mix. Draw up prescribed dose.
  4. Gently tent the skin on the side of the neck or between shoulder blades.
  5. Insert needle at a 45‑degree angle, push plunger slowly.
  6. Withdraw needle, gently massage the area. Reward with a treat.

5. Common Complications & When to Call the Vet

  • Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar): Weakness, stumbling, seizures, collapse. Immediately rub honey or corn syrup on gums, then call vet.
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA): Vomiting, dehydration, rapid breathing, lethargy. Requires emergency care.
  • Insulin resistance: High doses needed; often due to concurrent illness (urinary tract infection, dental disease).

7. Your 3‑Step Action Plan

  1. Schedule a veterinary appointment if you notice excessive thirst, urination, or weight loss.
  2. Learn insulin injection technique – practice with saline on a toy if nervous.
  3. Invest in home glucose monitoring – an AlphaTrak meter or Freestyle Libre saves money and improves safety.

Final thought: Diabetes is not a death sentence. With consistent insulin, a proper diet, and home monitoring, diabetic pets live long, happy lives.
