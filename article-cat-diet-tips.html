1. Cats Are Obligate Carnivores – Here's Why It Matters

Unlike dogs (who are omnivores), cats have evolved to eat almost exclusively animal tissue. Their bodies lack specific enzymes to efficiently digest plant matter. Key differences:

  • Require preformed vitamin A – cannot convert beta‑carotene from plants.
  • Need arachidonic acid – an omega‑6 fatty acid found only in animal fats.
  • High protein requirement – minimum 26% dry matter, but optimal 40‑50%.
  • Low thirst drive – evolved to get most water from prey (70‑80% moisture).

Feeding a diet that violates these biological needs leads to chronic dehydration, muscle wasting, and organ stress.

2. How to Decode a Cat Food Label (Ignore the Front, Read the Back)

The front of the bag is advertising: "natural," "premium," "holistic" – meaningless terms. Flip to the ingredient list and guaranteed analysis.

Rule 1: First ingredient must be a named meat

"Chicken" or "salmon" – good. "Chicken meal" also acceptable (concentrated protein). Avoid "chicken by‑product meal" (low‑quality scraps) and any grain or vegetable as first ingredient.

Rule 2: Protein percentage (dry matter basis)

For wet food: minimum 10% crude protein as fed. For dry food: minimum 35% crude protein as fed. Subtract moisture to calculate dry matter: if a wet food has 10% protein and 78% moisture, the dry matter protein = 10 / (1 - 0.78) = 45% (excellent).

Rule 3: Avoid unnecessary fillers

Corn, wheat, soy, rice, potatoes, tapioca – cats have no biological need for these. They add calories without nutrition and can spike blood sugar.

5. What About Homemade or Raw Diets?

Proceed with extreme caution. A 2019 UC Davis study found that 94% of homemade cat recipes were nutritionally deficient – missing essential amino acids (taurine), vitamins (B1, E), or minerals (calcium, phosphorus). Raw meat also carries bacterial risk (Salmonella, E. coli) for both cats and humans.

If you want to cook for your cat, work with a veterinary nutritionist to formulate a balanced recipe. Never guess.

7. Your 3‑Step Action Plan

  1. Calculate your cat's ideal weight and current food portion. Use a kitchen scale – not a cup measure.
  2. Switch to a high‑protein, low‑carb food (one dry, one wet) over 5‑7 days to avoid stomach upset.
  3. Replace plastic bowls with stainless steel and add a water fountain if your cat drinks little.
